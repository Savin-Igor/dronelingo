## The first question: who is the operator here?

In simple work, the answer is obvious. In shared operations, it often is not.

Ask:

- who owns or controls the aircraft?
- who plans and authorises the mission?
- who provides the pilot?
- whose insurance policy names the activity?

The invoice alone does not answer the insurance question. The real operating structure does.

## Subcontract pilots create hidden gaps

A common mistake is assuming any qualified pilot may fly under the operator's insurance automatically. Some policies are broad enough. Some are not.

Before a subcontract or second pilot flies:

- confirm whether the policy covers any authorised pilot or only named persons;
- confirm whether commercial subcontracting is included;
- document who had operational control of the mission.

## Renting or borrowing aircraft

When the aircraft is not yours, separate three questions:

- who covers **third-party liability**?
- who covers **damage to the aircraft**?
- who covers **the attached payload**?

If the answer to any of those is vague, the arrangement is weak.

## Cross-border work inside the EU

Even where a Latvian policy is generally valid, cross-border work still requires checking:

- whether local operational requirements add conditions;
- whether the insurer needs to be informed about the territory or work type;
- whether client contract language creates obligations beyond the policy.

<Scenario title="A Latvian operator wins a short commercial job in another EU country. The aircraft belongs to a partner company, and a freelance pilot will fly it under your project. What must be clear before the mission starts?">
Three things must be clear:

1. who is the legal operator for that mission;
2. whose policies cover liability, hull, and payload;
3. whether the freelance pilot is actually covered under the intended policy structure.

If any of these are assumed instead of verified, the operator is building risk into the contract before takeoff.
</Scenario>
