## Why documentation matters

After an incident, three different parties may ask for evidence:

- **the insurer** — to decide whether the claim is covered;
- **the regulator** — to understand whether reporting duties apply;
- **the client** — to assess delay, damage, or data loss.

If the operator has poor records, the same event becomes harder to defend in all three directions.

## The three records that save time later

**1. A flight log.**  
Date, place, pilot, aircraft, purpose, batteries used, anomalies, and whether the flight completed normally.

**2. A battery log.**  
Battery identity, cycle count, swelling, storage issues, unusual heating, and retirement date.

**3. A maintenance log.**  
Prop changes, firmware state, repairs, payload changes, and any known limitation.

These logs are boring until something goes wrong. After that, they become the fastest way to show that the operator was organised and the aircraft was not being used blindly.

## What to do immediately after an incident

1. make the site safe first;
2. preserve the aircraft and damaged parts;
3. take photos before moving too much;
4. export or save digital logs and screenshots early;
5. write down the timeline while memory is fresh;
6. notify insurer, regulator, or client as required.

## Do not “clean up the story”

A common mistake is to tidy the aircraft, recharge batteries, or rewrite the timeline before documenting. That destroys evidence and weakens both safety learning and claim credibility.

<Scenario title="A drone clips a light pole during a low-speed approach. No one is injured, but one arm, the gimbal, and a battery are damaged. The pilot wants to pack up quickly because the client is waiting. What should happen first?">
First secure the site and make sure no secondary hazard remains. Then document before cleanup:

- photo the aircraft where it stopped, if safe;
- capture the pole, surroundings, and impact marks;
- record the battery ID and warning state;
- save flight logs and screenshots from the app;
- note witness details and exact time.

Packing fast may feel efficient, but it destroys the evidence needed for insurance, reporting, and later learning.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Recharging or swapping the battery before documenting its condition.
- Repairing or cleaning the aircraft before photographing the damage.
- Keeping flight logs only inside the app and not exporting them after an incident.
- Writing notes hours later from memory instead of immediately on site.
- Treating the client update as more urgent than preserving the evidence package.
</CommonMistakes>
