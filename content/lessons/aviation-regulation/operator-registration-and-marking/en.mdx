## Three identifiers, three purposes

In the Open category, pilots need to keep these separate:

- **pilot competency** — proves the person may operate in the relevant category;
- **operator registration number** — identifies the legal operator responsible for the aircraft;
- **class marking** — identifies what technical class the aircraft belongs to.

The most common compliance failure is treating the pilot certificate as if it replaced operator registration. It does not.

## When operator registration is required

As a rule, the operator must register when operating a drone that is **not just a simple toy-level C0 case**, especially where the aircraft has a sensor capable of capturing personal data or falls outside the lowest exempt conditions.

For practical A1/A3 exam logic, the safest mental model is:

- if the aircraft is more than the simplest exempt low-risk case, **assume operator registration is required**;
- the operator number must then be attached to the aircraft as required by the applicable rules;
- the number identifies the operator, not the pilot physically holding the controller that day.

## Where the number goes

The operator number must be placed so that the aircraft can be linked back to the responsible operator. In practical terms:

- keep it **readable and durable**;
- place it where routine handling will not immediately remove it;
- ensure the aircraft and any remote identification configuration use the correct operator identity where required.

If several aircraft belong to the same operator, the same operator registration may appear on all of them. The number follows the operator entity, not one specific airframe.

## Why this matters in enforcement

Registration solves the accountability question: **who is responsible for this aircraft being in the air?** It does not prove that the pilot is skilled, and it does not prove that the aircraft is in the right class. That is why all three identifiers coexist.

<Scenario title="A small company owns three drones. One employee holds the A1/A3 certificate and another employee flies under company procedures for a photo mission. Which identity must appear on the aircraft?">
The aircraft must carry the **operator registration number of the company**, because the company is the legal operator. The pilot certificate belongs to the person flying. The class mark belongs to the aircraft itself.

This distinction matters especially in commercial operations, where several pilots may fly aircraft owned and managed by one operator entity.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Putting only the pilot certificate details in the flight file and forgetting the operator identity on the aircraft.
- Assuming each drone needs a completely different operator identity even when all belong to the same operator.
- Confusing the class mark with registration and treating the C-label as proof of legal operator identification.
- Using temporary, easily removable stickers that do not survive routine field handling.
</CommonMistakes>
