## The legal stack in one picture

For an A1/A3 pilot, the legal logic works in layers:

1. **EU baseline**: Regulation 2019/947 defines operations; Regulation 2019/945 defines classes and technical product rules.
2. **Latvian implementation**: the Aviation Law and Cabinet regulations set local execution rules for qualification, insurance, registry, and specific-category administration.
3. **Operational portals**: `e.caa.gov.lv` handles operator registration and BGKIS notifications; `airspace.lv/drones` is the practical source for checking whether a place is legal to fly.

If EU law tells you *what category you are in*, Latvian rules tell you *what local steps you must complete before takeoff*.

## The two portals every Latvia-first pilot must know

**`e.caa.gov.lv`** is the CAA Latvia portal. In Open category you use it for:

- operator registration
- exam and qualification flows
- BGKIS notifications for restricted UAS geographical zones
- occurrence reporting

**`airspace.lv/drones`** is the operational map. It answers the immediate field question: *may I fly here, under what conditions, and do I need a BGKIS notification?*

These portals are complementary. `airspace.lv` tells you the zone logic; `e.caa.gov.lv` is where you perform the regulated action.

## A practical Open-category workflow in Latvia

For a normal flight in Latvia, the sequence is:

1. confirm your operator registration is valid
2. confirm you personally hold the needed competence level
3. confirm insurance if your UAS and operation require it
4. check `airspace.lv/drones` for the location
5. submit BGKIS in `e.caa.gov.lv` if the zone requires it
6. fly only inside the approved window and still remain inside intrinsic Open-category limits

That last point is load-bearing: **BGKIS approval does not let you exceed Open-category limits**. It does not authorise BVLOS, overflight of assemblies, or routine flight above 120 m AGL.
