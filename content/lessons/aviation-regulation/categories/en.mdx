# Open, Specific and Certified categories

## Why three categories?

EU Regulation 2019/947 groups all drone operations by risk. The higher the risk, the more authorisation is required before a single flight can take place.

## Open — the category you are studying

Open is the lowest-risk tier. It applies when all of the following are true simultaneously:

- the UAS is under 25 kg (MTOM);
- the pilot maintains VLOS throughout;
- the drone stays below 120 m AGL;
- no dangerous goods are carried;
- no flight over assemblies of people.

If even one condition fails, the operation is no longer in Open category.

Open subdivides into **A1, A2, A3** — each with specific UAS class requirements and distance-to-people rules. The A1/A3 exam qualifies you to fly in A1 (with C0 and C1 drones) and A3 (with C2, C3, C4, and legacy drones).

## Specific — when Open is not enough

Specific category covers operations that exceed Open limits: BVLOS flights, flights above 120 m, or flights with heavier UAS near or over people. The operator must either:

- follow a **Standard Scenario (STS)** — a pre-defined risk package that comes with a declaration, or
- conduct a full **SORA (Specific Operations Risk Assessment)** and obtain an operating permit from the CAA.

The cost for a Latvian Specific category permit starts at €175 (PDRA-S01) and rises with operational complexity.

## Certified — aviation-grade operations

Certified applies to the highest-risk operations: carrying passengers, high-speed urban delivery, flights over dense populations with large drones. Both the UAS and the operator must hold individual certifications — similar to manned aviation. Certified is outside the scope of the A1/A3 exam entirely.

<Scenario title="A client asks you to fly a camera drone continuously at 800 m altitude along a pipeline to inspect for leaks. No people are below. Which category applies?">
**Specific category.** The flight exceeds 120 m AGL, which takes it outside Open regardless of the empty terrain below. The operator would need either an STS that covers this scenario or a SORA-based operating permit.

The A1/A3 certificate does not authorise this flight.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Thinking the A1/A3 certificate covers any type of flight under 25 kg — the category limits still apply.
- Confusing Open sub-categories (A1, A2, A3) with the category (Open) — the exam tests both levels.
- Believing that flying in an unpopulated area removes the 120 m ceiling — it does not.
- Assuming BVLOS is allowed with any Open certificate — BVLOS always requires Specific category or higher.
</CommonMistakes>
