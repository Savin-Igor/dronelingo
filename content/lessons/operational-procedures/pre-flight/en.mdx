## Why a structured check matters

A pre-flight check is not bureaucracy. It is a memory aid for the 12 things that can go wrong before the drone leaves the ground — and the few that cause incidents the moment it does.

The structure matters. Doing the checks in a random order means you will skip something under time pressure. A consistent sequence becomes a habit; habits survive distraction.

## Environment — three questions before touching the drone

**Weather.** Wind speed and direction must be within the UAS manufacturer's specifications. Visibility must allow VLOS at your planned maximum distance. No rain, snow, or ice. Check a forecast for the area, not just the launch point — conditions can differ 2 km away.

**UGZ and NOTAMs.** Open airspace.lv/drones. If the zone is restricted and you do not have a confirmed BGKIS approval, the flight does not happen. Also check for any temporary NOTAM — airshows, military exercises, emergency operations — that may have appeared since you last looked.

**Surroundings.** Walk the site. Identify obstacles in the planned flight path (power lines are harder to see than trees). Identify where uninvolved persons could appear and how you would respond. Confirm there is a safe emergency landing area.

## Pilot — last check before takeoff

Run IMSAFE. If any item is "yes" — stop here. The environment and drone may be perfect. An impaired pilot erases both advantages.

<Scenario title="You arrive at a commercial shoot location. It is raining lightly. The drone is IPX4 rated (splash-proof). The client says 'other drones fly in light rain all the time'. What do you do?">
**Ground the flight.** IPX4 protects against splashing from any direction — not sustained rain. "Other drones do it" is not a regulatory or manufacturer authorisation. Rain also degrades VLOS and makes surfaces slippery.

The pre-flight environment check — weather — failed. The manufacturer specification likely states "do not fly in rain." The client's opinion about what other pilots do is not part of the pre-flight checklist.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Skipping the zone check because "I fly here all the time" — zones change without notice.
- Setting RTH altitude from habit (e.g. always 50 m) instead of the actual obstacle height on the return path.
- Skipping compass calibration after driving to a new location — metal in the car can disturb the compass.
- Not identifying a safe emergency landing area before the first motor starts.
- Running hardware checks without the drone powered on — some warnings only appear on the live screen.
</CommonMistakes>
