## Why post-flight matters

Post-flight is where you convert a flight from a one-off event into usable operational learning.

Without a shutdown routine:

- hot batteries go straight into bags or cars
- small propeller damage is discovered only at the next launch
- GPS or control anomalies are forgotten instead of documented
- a reportable occurrence becomes an unreported memory

The goal is simple: leave the aircraft, the batteries, and the record in a state that makes the next flight safer than the last one.

## The minimum useful log entry

A useful post-flight log is short. It should answer:

- when and where the flight happened
- which UAS and battery were used
- whether any anomaly appeared
- whether maintenance or a follow-up check is needed

You are not writing a novel. You are preserving operational memory for the next flight.

## Batteries after landing

The most common mistake is treating a just-landed battery like a neutral object. It is not. After a demanding flight the pack may be warm internally even if the outer shell feels only mildly hot.

Safe sequence:

1. remove the battery when the aircraft is stable and powered down
2. place it on a fire-safe, non-flammable surface
3. allow it to cool before charging
4. if the next flight is not soon, return it to storage charge rather than leaving it full

Never leave a fully charged battery in a hot car after a summer mission.

## Reportable or just logged?

Not every anomaly is a mandatory report, but every anomaly is worth logging.

Use this split:

- **Log only**: small vibration, short GNSS warning that resolved cleanly, minor propeller nick found after landing
- **Escalate for occurrence reporting**: serious loss of control, near-miss with manned aircraft, GPS spoofing/jamming affecting safety, injury, or any event that materially reduced safety margins

When in doubt, preserve evidence: screenshots, timestamps, location, and a short factual description.

<Scenario title="You land after a short urban practice flight. The battery feels warm, one propeller has a shallow chip, and the app log shows a brief compass warning at minute 3 that disappeared on its own. What is the correct post-flight action?">
Do not pack and leave immediately. Set the battery aside to cool, replace the damaged propeller before the next flight, and write a short log entry that records the compass warning, time, and location.

This is not automatically a mandatory occurrence report, but it is absolutely operationally relevant. If the same warning appears again on the next flight, your log turns two isolated “small things” into a pattern.
</Scenario>
