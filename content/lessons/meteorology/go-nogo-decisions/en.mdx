# Weather go/no-go decisions

<MissionBriefing objective="After this lesson you will apply a structured go/no-go weather framework before every flight, check the correct Latvian weather sources, and know the wind limits that different drone classes impose.">

## Weather sources for Latvia

Before every flight, check **two independent sources**:

**Primary — observation:**
- **meteo.lv** (Latvian Environment, Geology and Meteorology Centre — LVGMC): official Latvian weather service. Provides current observations, hourly forecasts, and aviation weather (METAR/TAF for Latvian airports).
- **aviationweather.gov** (NOAA): global METAR and TAF, useful for cross-referencing.

**For aviation-specific formats:**
- METAR: current conditions at airport observation stations.
- **TAF** (Terminal Aerodrome Forecast): 24–30 hour forecast for an airport in the same format as METAR. Published every 6 hours (0000, 0600, 1200, 1800 UTC).

Do not rely solely on the weather widget in the manufacturer's app — it typically shows a commercial forecast API, not an aviation-grade observation.

## Reading a TAF

Example TAF: `EVRA 101700Z 1018/1118 27012KT 9999 FEW020 BECMG 1022/1024 29018KT`

| Field | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| EVRA | Riga airport |
| 101700Z | Issued at 17:00 UTC on the 10th |
| 1018/1118 | Valid from 10th 18:00 to 11th 18:00 UTC |
| 27012KT 9999 FEW020 | Base conditions: wind 270°/12 KT, vis > 10 km, few clouds at 2,000 ft |
| BECMG 1022/1024 | Becoming (gradual change) between 22:00 and 24:00 |
| 29018KT | Wind changing to 290°/18 KT |

**BECMG** = gradual change. **TEMPO** = temporary fluctuations expected for less than half of the stated period. **FM** = from a specific time, conditions change completely.

## Wind limits by drone class

The manufacturer specifies the maximum wind speed in the UAS manual. Always use that value. For reference, common class-based practical limits:

| Typical class / size | Practical wind limit (mean wind) |
|---------------------|----------------------------------|
| C0 mini (< 250 g) | 5–8 m/s |
| C1 mid-size (< 900 g) | 8–12 m/s |
| C2 (< 4 kg) | 10–14 m/s |
| C3 industrial (< 25 kg) | 12–18 m/s |

These are mean wind limits — subtract 20–30 % for gusty conditions to stay within safe operating margins. A drone rated at 12 m/s in gusts of 18 m/s is overloaded.

## The go/no-go framework

Work through this sequence **before every flight**:

1. **Wind within specs?**
   - Check METAR or current observation for mean wind AND gusts.
   - Compare against manufacturer limit.
   - If gusts exceed the limit: **NO-GO**.

2. **Visibility adequate for VLOS?**
   - Is the visibility sufficient to keep continuous unaided visual contact and judge the drone’s attitude and direction at the planned range?
   - The regulation gives no fixed visibility number; the test is functional VLOS.
   - If haze, fog or low contrast would put the drone near the edge of visibility: **NO-GO**.

3. **Precipitation?**
   - Rain, snow, sleet, freezing drizzle: **NO-GO** for all UAS not certified for precipitation.
   - IPX ratings protect against splashing, not sustained precipitation.

4. **Active thunderstorm close to the area?**
   - Check radar (meteo.lv radar tab) for active Cb cells near the route or moving toward it.
   - A conservative training rule is to treat any thunderstorm within roughly 10 km as **NO-GO**.
   - Any visible lightning anywhere: **NO-GO**.

5. **Temperature near 0°C + moisture?**
   - Icing risk: **NO-GO**.

If all five pass: **GO** — but monitor continuously and be ready to abort.

## Wind at altitude vs. ground

Surface wind is measured 10 m above ground. At typical drone operating heights the wind is often stronger than at the surface. This matters for:
- RTH — the drone must fly into the wind at altitude, which drains the battery faster than expected.
- Filming — the drone fights wind while hovering, reducing stability and image quality.
- Emergency battery margins — the headwind on return consumes more power than the tailwind on outbound.

Check the wind at altitude if the forecast provides it (some NWP services show wind at 100 m). If not, leave a clear margin rather than assuming the surface reading will stay the same aloft.

<Scenario title="Pre-flight check: METAR shows wind 22008KT. Your drone is rated at 10 m/s. TAF shows TEMPO 1012/1014 29018G28KT. Your planned flight is 11:00–12:00. Can you go?">
**NO-GO for the planned window.** The TAF TEMPO group overlaps the flight window and includes gusts above the drone limit.

The current METAR is within limits, but a temporary deterioration is forecast for the time you intend to fly. Treat that as operationally relevant, wait until the TEMPO window has passed, and confirm the improvement with a fresh observation before launching.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Checking weather at the last minute with no time to reschedule if conditions fail — check 24 h ahead for the next day's forecast.
- Using the manufacturer app weather widget as the sole weather check — it is not an aviation-grade source.
- Not checking the TAF for conditions during the planned flight window, only checking the current METAR — current conditions may be fine while a deterioration is forecast.
- Treating TEMPO as harmless because it is temporary — a temporary deterioration inside your flight window still matters.
- Ignoring altitude wind — assuming ground conditions equal flight-level conditions.
</CommonMistakes>
