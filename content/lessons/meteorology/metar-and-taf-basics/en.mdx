## What these reports are

**METAR** is an observation report: what the weather was at a specific aerodrome at a specific time.

**TAF** is a forecast: what the weather is expected to be over a defined period.

A drone pilot does not use them as a perfect description of the launch point. The value is different: they provide a structured, aviation-grade picture of wind, gusts, visibility, and change over time.

## The parts that matter most to UAS pilots

For most Open-category decisions, these fields matter first:

- **wind direction and speed**
- **gusts**
- **visibility**
- **significant cloud / storm cues**
- **forecast trend in your flight window**

## A practical reading habit

Do not try to decode everything at once. Read in this order:

1. wind and gusts
2. visibility
3. precipitation or thunderstorm clues
4. time trend during your planned launch and recovery window

Then compare that weather picture with:

- your planned site
- your planned distance and altitude
- the aircraft limits in the manual

## Why drone pilots should care about airport weather

The nearest aerodrome is not your exact field, but it is usually much better than guessing.

Airport weather helps you avoid two bad habits:

- underestimating gusts because the launch point “feels fine”
- treating weather as static instead of trend-based

METAR and TAF are decision aids, not perfect truth. They should sharpen judgment, not replace looking outside.

<Scenario title="The nearest aerodrome METAR shows `18012G22KT`, and your drone manual gives a maximum demonstrated wind of 10 m/s. The site itself feels calm at launch point level because it is partially sheltered by buildings. What is the correct interpretation?">
The sheltered launch point is not enough evidence to ignore the METAR. Gusts to 22 knots indicate a real risk that the UAS may encounter much stronger wind once it climbs above the local shelter or moves out of the lee of buildings.

The correct response is conservative: treat the gust forecast as operationally relevant and make a strict go/no-go decision against the aircraft limit, not against how comfortable the ground feels.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Reading only sustained wind and ignoring gusts.
- Using METAR as if it were an exact measurement of the takeoff spot.
- Treating a single calm moment as proof that the full flight window is safe.
- Looking at weather after planning the mission instead of before.
</CommonMistakes>
