## Wind — the primary weather hazard

Wind is the most frequent weather cause of UAS incidents. Key concepts:

**Mean wind speed** — the average speed over a period (typically 10 minutes). Your drone's manual specifies a maximum mean wind speed. Exceeding it reduces control authority and accelerates battery drain.

**Gusts** — brief peaks above the mean wind. A forecast of "15 kt gusting 25 kt" means momentary loads 67 % higher than the mean. Drones handle gusts poorly because their autopilots must react faster than the gust rises.

**Turbulence** — irregular, chaotic airflow caused by:
- **Mechanical turbulence**: wind flowing over buildings, trees, terrain — creates unpredictable eddies downwind.
- **Thermal turbulence**: warm ground heats air which rises in columns (thermals), cooler air sinks — most intense in the early afternoon on sunny days.
- **Shear turbulence**: rapid change in wind speed or direction between altitude layers (wind shear).

**Wind shear** is particularly dangerous for UAS because it happens at the transition between layers — a drone climbing from a calm ground layer into a 15 m/s layer aloft can be flipped or rapidly displaced.

## Reading a METAR

A **METAR** (Meteorological Aerodrome Report) is a standardised weather observation issued at airports, typically every 30 or 60 minutes. It is the most reliable current weather source for a specific location.

Example: `METAR EVRA 101150Z 27012KT 9999 FEW020 14/08 Q1015`

| Field | Value | Meaning |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Station | EVRA | Riga airport ICAO code |
| Time | 101150Z | 10th day, 11:50 UTC (Zulu) |
| Wind | 27012KT | Wind from 270° (west) at 12 knots |
| Visibility | 9999 | ≥ 10 km (maximum reportable) |
| Cloud | FEW020 | Few clouds at 2,000 ft AGL (≈ 600 m) |
| Temp/Dew | 14/08 | Temperature 14 °C, dew point 8 °C |
| QNH | Q1015 | Altimeter setting 1015 hPa |

Wind in METAR is given in **knots** (KT). 1 knot ≈ 0.51 m/s. A wind of 12 KT ≈ 6 m/s.

## Density altitude and why hot days are harder

Air density decreases with higher temperature and lower atmospheric pressure. At lower air density:
- Propellers generate less lift per revolution — the drone must spin faster for the same thrust.
- Maximum payload capacity decreases.
- Battery drain increases (motors work harder).
- Maximum wind resistance decreases.

**High density altitude conditions**: summer heat, low pressure, high elevation above sea level. A drone rated for 10 m/s wind in standard conditions may only handle 7–8 m/s on a hot summer day.

## Temperature inversion

Normally, air temperature decreases with altitude. A **temperature inversion** reverses this: a layer of warmer air sits above cooler surface air. This traps:
- Pollution and haze near the surface, reducing visibility.
- Moist air, which can cause fog or low cloud.
- The inversion layer itself acts as a "lid" that intensifies turbulence below it.

Inversions are common in Latvia in autumn and early winter, particularly in the morning.

<Scenario title="You arrive at a coastal shoot location. The METAR for the nearby airport shows wind 18012G22KT — 12 knots gusting 22. Your drone's manual specifies a maximum of 10 m/s (≈ 20 KT). Can you fly?">
**No — the gusts exceed the drone's limit.** 22 KT = approximately 11.3 m/s, which exceeds the 10 m/s maximum. Even if the mean wind (12 KT / 6.2 m/s) is within limits, gusts impose peak loads that can destabilise the aircraft and cause control loss.

Wait for conditions to improve, move to a sheltered location if the specific site is more turbulent than the airport, or reschedule. The drone's maximum wind limit is not a guideline — it is the engineering boundary.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Using smartphone weather apps instead of METAR for precise pre-flight assessment — apps show forecasts, METAR shows observations.
- Ignoring gust values and only checking mean wind — gusts cause the actual overloads.
- Not accounting for mechanical turbulence near buildings — conditions at the launch point may be calm while the planned flight path is turbulent.
- Flying in summer heat at full payload without checking manufacturer hot-weather derating — performance degrades more than expected.
- Not checking wind at altitude vs. at the ground — surface wind can be calm while conditions aloft are significantly stronger.
</CommonMistakes>
