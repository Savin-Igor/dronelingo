## What a NOTAM actually does

A **NOTAM** is a time-limited operational notice. It can create, modify, or cancel restrictions for a specific period. For a drone pilot, this means:

- an area that was usable yesterday may be restricted today;
- the ceiling may be temporarily reduced;
- emergency, police, military, or public-event activity may change who has priority;
- a previously valid routine plan may become invalid without any change in your drone or certificate.

The practical rule is simple: **do not rely on memory for airspace status**.

## Typical temporary restrictions in Latvia

The most common short-notice changes for UAS pilots are:

- **public events** — concerts, sports, city festivals, state visits;
- **emergency services activity** — fire, search and rescue, police scene management;
- **military activity** — temporary reserved or restricted areas;
- **airport operational changes** — runway works, traffic flow changes, special helicopter routes.

These restrictions may appear on `airspace.lv`, in official notices, or as zone-manager instructions linked to the operating area.

## The right workflow

Use this sequence for every real operation:

1. **Plan in advance** — identify normal zone type and likely constraints.
2. **Re-check on the flight day** — preferably shortly before travel and again on site.
3. **Read the time window carefully** — many temporary restrictions are valid only for several hours.
4. **Treat temporary restrictions as primary** — if a temporary rule conflicts with your routine assumption, the temporary rule wins.
5. **If in doubt, do not improvise** — cancel or delay until the zone status is clear.

## Why “it looked green earlier” is not a defense

CAA and airspace authorities evaluate the actual conditions at the time of flight, not what the pilot remembers from a previous check. A screenshot from yesterday is not operational compliance for today.

<Scenario title="You planned a weekend real-estate shoot on Friday. The area was clear then. On Sunday morning, `airspace.lv` shows a temporary restricted area because of a city event nearby. The client is already on location. What is the correct decision?">
The correct decision is to follow the **current** status, not the earlier plan.

If the temporary restriction requires BGKIS approval and you do not have it, you do not fly. If the temporary restriction makes the area prohibited, you cancel immediately. Client pressure does not change the airspace status.

The professional response is short and factual: the operating environment changed, and aviation rules are based on the active conditions at the time of flight.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Planning once and treating the airspace picture as fixed for the whole week.
- Reading only the zone colour and skipping the active time window.
- Assuming a temporary event restriction is “only for manned aviation.”
- Relying on client urgency instead of live operational status.
- Saving the map offline but not refreshing it before the mission.
</CommonMistakes>
