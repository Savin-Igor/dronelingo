## Workload is cumulative

One task alone is rarely the issue. Workload rises when several demands overlap:

- flying precisely,
- watching people,
- checking wind,
- answering a client,
- framing a shot,
- dealing with warnings.

The danger is not just that the pilot feels busy. The danger is that judgement quality falls while confidence often stays high.

## Why fixed decision gates work

A decision gate is a moment you define **before** pressure rises. That matters because people make worse decisions when improvising under time stress than when applying a rule chosen in advance.

For example:

- if wind exceeds your personal limit, you do not debate it on site;
- if bystanders enter the planned area, you pause and reset the area;
- if the client keeps changing the shot during landing, you ignore the request and complete the landing first.

## Signs that workload is already too high

Watch for these cues:

- you stop scanning and stare at one thing;
- you miss simple checklist steps;
- you hear instructions but do not process them well;
- you start rushing instead of simplifying;
- your task feels “urgent” even though the aircraft is still in a controllable state.

When these signs appear, the right move is usually not to push harder. It is to reduce the number of active tasks.

<Scenario title="You are flying a short commercial orbit around a building. The client is asking for framing changes, wind increases near the corner, battery drops faster than expected, and one pedestrian enters the edge of the area. What should happen next?">
This is not one problem. It is a workload stack.

The correct response is to stop adding tasks: abandon framing discussion, move to a simpler safe position, reassess the pedestrian and wind, and if needed pause or land. The mission should only continue after the workload returns to a manageable level.
</Scenario>
