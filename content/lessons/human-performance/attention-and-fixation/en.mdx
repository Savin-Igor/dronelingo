## Why good pilots still miss things

UAS pilots do not fail only because they are careless. They often fail because they are *too focused on one thing*:

- framing the shot
- watching battery percentage
- reading the map
- solving a warning message

That is called **fixation**. Attention collapses onto one task and stops scanning the rest of the environment.

In drone flying, fixation is dangerous because the situation changes outside the controller screen:

- people enter the area
- a helicopter appears
- wind shifts near obstacles
- the aircraft drifts while the pilot is head-down

## Two classic traps

**Empty-field myopia.** In a visually simple environment, the eyes relax and stop actively refocusing. The pilot thinks they are “looking outside,” but visual detection quality drops.

**Tunnel vision.** Under stress or task overload, attention narrows. FPV, live video work, and client pressure make this worse.

## A practical scan loop

Use a short, repeatable loop:

1. confirm where the aircraft is and what it is doing
2. scan the surrounding sky and approach paths
3. scan people, vehicles, and obstacles near the operating area
4. glance at controller data only long enough to answer a specific question
5. return outside immediately

The point is not to stare everywhere. The point is to **avoid being trapped anywhere**.

## Why screens make this worse

A controller screen gives the brain a false sense of certainty. It looks rich, precise, and “complete.” But it shows only selected data. It does not show what is behind you, beside you, or about to enter the area.

This is why a pilot can be technically informed and situationally blind at the same time.

<Scenario title="You are landing after a practice session. At three metres AGL, a friend starts asking where to stand for a photo. You look down at the controller to answer and adjust camera angle. What is the real risk?">
The real risk is not the question itself. The real risk is **fixation during a critical phase**. At low altitude there is little time margin. Looking down at the controller and changing camera settings steals the exact attention needed for descent, drift correction, and separation from people.

The correct response is to ignore the non-essential question, complete the landing, and resume conversation only after motors are stopped.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Calling prolonged screen-watching “situational awareness.”
- Trying to solve non-critical settings mid-flight instead of flying first and configuring later.
- Letting client conversation or bystanders interrupt low-altitude phases.
- Believing that because the area looked empty 30 seconds ago, it is still empty now.
</CommonMistakes>
