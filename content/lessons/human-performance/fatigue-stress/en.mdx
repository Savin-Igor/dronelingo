# Fatigue and stress

<MissionBriefing objective="After this lesson you will understand how fatigue degrades pilot performance, recognise the three types of stress that affect UAS operations, and know when to stop.">
You can pre-flight a drone in 3 minutes. You cannot pre-flight your brain. Fatigue is invisible to everyone except the person experiencing it — and they are usually the last to notice.
</MissionBriefing>

## What fatigue actually does

At 16 hours without sleep, cognitive performance has degraded to a level equivalent to a blood-alcohol concentration of 0.5 ‰. At 24 hours, the equivalent is approximately 1.0 ‰.

In Latvia, the legal limit for driving is 0.5 ‰. A rested drone pilot who drinks to that limit cannot legally drive — or safely fly. The same limit applies to sleep deprivation, with no breathalyser required and no external check available.

The symptoms are: slowed reaction, narrowed attention, impaired risk assessment, and — critically — **reduced ability to recognise that you are impaired**.

## Three types of stress

**Acute stress** appears in the moment: a client running late, a hardware problem at the job site, unexpected gusts while the drone is airborne. Acute stress contracts your attention to the immediate threat and makes it harder to see hazards outside that narrow focus.

**Chronic stress** accumulates over days and weeks: heavy workload, poor sleep patterns, personal circumstances. It makes the baseline worse even before a flight begins.

**Technical stress** is specific to UAS operations: repeated BGKIS rejections, GPS signal loss near the eastern border, an unfamiliar drone model. Even experienced pilots encounter it when something new goes wrong.

## The three-question self-test

Before flight, ask three plain questions:

1. **Can I make a safe decision under time pressure right now?**
2. **Is my attention on this flight — or on several other things?**
3. **Am I ready to abort the mission if something goes wrong?**

If any answer is "no" — postpone. If the answer is "I think so" — that is a "no."

<Scenario title="You have a commercial shoot booked. The client is watching from the field. You slept badly, you are behind schedule, and the battery you are using is the one you are less sure about. What should you do?">
Three active risk factors: fatigue (poor sleep), acute stress (waiting client, schedule pressure), technical concern (uncertain battery).

The correct action is to **pause and address each factor** before the first motor spins:
1. Tell the client honestly: "I'm going to take 5 minutes to check the battery and confirm my setup."
2. Swap the uncertain battery for a known-good one if available.
3. Take 60 seconds of stillness before starting — reset cognitive baseline.

If the fatigue is severe enough to impair judgment, the correct action is to postpone and reschedule. A professional who grounds a flight is safer, not weaker.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Flying after a long workday "because it's just a quick job" — fatigue does not care about flight duration.
- Letting a paying client's presence override the go/no-go decision — the pilot's call is not negotiable.
- Confusing caffeine for rest — stimulants mask fatigue without restoring cognitive performance.
- Ignoring chronic stress as "not a safety issue" — chronic fatigue degrades baseline before the IMSAFE check.
- Not filing voluntary occurrence reports after stress-related near-misses — the pattern never gets fixed.
</CommonMistakes>
