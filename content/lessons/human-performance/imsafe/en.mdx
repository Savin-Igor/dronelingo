## Why the pilot is the weakest link

An Open-category drone pilot has no co-pilot, no air traffic controller monitoring their performance, no technical crew double-checking the setup. If the pilot's judgment is off today, nobody else will catch it.

IMSAFE is borrowed from manned aviation — where the stakes of a degraded pilot are obvious. For UAS operations the stakes are lower, but the principle is identical: **assess yourself before you assess the aircraft.**

## The six checks

**I — Illness.** Any active infection degrades cognitive performance and, in the case of respiratory illness, may force you to focus on your own discomfort rather than the flight. Even a mild cold slows your reactions.

**M — Medication.** Over-the-counter antihistamines, decongestants, and painkillers all have CNS effects. Read the label. If it says "may cause drowsiness" — it will.

**S — Stress.** Acute emotional stress (an argument, bad news, financial pressure) consumes exactly the cognitive bandwidth that safe UAS operation requires. Stress is not an excuse. It is a disqualifier.

**A — Alcohol.** EU aviation law adopts the same 8-hour "bottle to throttle" rule for UAS pilots. The blood-alcohol level after 8 hours is typically near zero — but judgment recovery lags behind blood clearance.

**F — Fatigue.** See the Fatigue lesson for the full picture. The practical check here: fewer than 6 hours of sleep the night before is a red flag; fewer than 5 is a disqualifier.

**E — Eating.** Hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar) causes tunnel vision, irritability, and slow decision-making. A flight without food in the preceding four hours is an unnecessary risk.

<Scenario title="You have a paid aerial photography job at 07:00. The client insisted on golden-hour light. You slept 4 hours, you have a slight headache (no medication), and you skipped breakfast to make it on time. IMSAFE?">
Three "yes" answers: Fatigue (4 hours sleep), possible Illness (headache — cause unknown), Eating (no breakfast).

**Ground the flight.** Explain to the client that the flight cannot safely proceed. A professional who grounds a flight for safety is more trustworthy — not less — than one who flies through self-evident warning signs.

Rescheduling costs time. An incident with a drone over a garden costs the client far more.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Treating IMSAFE as optional for short flights — fatigue and medication don't care about flight duration.
- Answering "probably fine" instead of yes or no — ambiguity defaults to yes.
- Skipping Eating as trivial — hypoglycaemia at 80 m altitude is a genuine risk.
- Thinking stress is a mental health issue, not a safety issue — they are the same thing in the cockpit context.
- Running IMSAFE once a week rather than before every individual flight.
</CommonMistakes>
