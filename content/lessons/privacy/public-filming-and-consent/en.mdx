## Flight legality and privacy legality are separate

A flight can be fully legal under airspace rules and still create a privacy problem.

These are different layers:

- **Airspace legality**: may I fly here?
- **Data-protection legality**: may I record, keep, share, or publish what I captured?

Public space does not cancel privacy law. It only means people were visible from a place open to others.

## Three practical routes

When people may be identifiable in the footage, the usual practical routes are:

1. **Consent**: you are filming specific people and they agree.
2. **Legitimate interest**: there is a real operational reason, and the privacy impact is limited and proportionate.
3. **Anonymisation**: you blur or otherwise remove identifiability before sharing or publishing.

If none of these routes works cleanly, the safest choice is to redesign the shot.

## The practical test: who is the subject of the shot?

Ask this before pressing record:

- Am I filming the place, or am I effectively filming the people in it?
- If a person appears, are they incidental or central?
- If I publish this, can a reasonable viewer identify someone?

The more the person becomes the real subject, the harder it is to justify publication without consent.

## Safer habits

- choose angles that reduce facial detail
- avoid hovering over café terraces, beaches, playgrounds, or queues if the people are visually central
- review footage before sharing, not after
- delete unusable personal-data-heavy footage instead of archiving it forever

<Scenario title="You film a city-promotion clip in a public square. The flight itself was lawful and the zone was open. In the best shot, several pedestrians are clearly identifiable and two car number plates are readable. What is the right publication decision?">
Do not publish the raw shot as-is. The lawful airspace operation does not create a free right to publish identifiable people and plates.

The correct route is to anonymise the clip first by blurring faces and plates, or replace it with a less privacy-sensitive shot.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Treating “public place” as if it were a GDPR exemption.
- Thinking a property owner can consent on behalf of random passers-by.
- Reviewing privacy risk only after publication planning has already started.
- Archiving raw footage indefinitely because “it might be useful one day.”
</CommonMistakes>
