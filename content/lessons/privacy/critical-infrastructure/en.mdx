## What counts as critical infrastructure

Latvia's Critical Infrastructure Protection Law designates specific categories of sites where unauthorised photography and filming is prohibited regardless of how the pilot arrived in the airspace above them:

- **Energy facilities** — power stations, substations, major transmission lines;
- **Water supply** — treatment plants, pumping stations, reservoirs;
- **Communications hubs** — major switching centres, data infrastructure;
- **Military sites** — all MoD-designated facilities and perimeter zones;
- **State border technical facilities** — surveillance and control equipment along the border.

Sites in these categories are marked with the sign:
> **"BEZ SASKAŅOŠANAS FOTOGRAFĒT, FILMĒT AIZLIEGTS"**
> *(Photography or filming forbidden without authorisation)*

## Why this is separate from UGZ

The Unified Geo Zone (UGZ) system controls **where a drone may fly**. The critical infrastructure filming ban controls **what the camera may record**. These are separate legal instruments enforced by separate authorities (CAA vs. relevant ministry / security services).

Consequences of conflating them:
- A site may sit outside any UGZ restricted zone — but the filming ban still applies.
- Obtaining BGKIS approval for the airspace does not grant filming rights.
- Airspace approval is granted by CAA Latvia. Filming authorisation is granted by the **site operator** (grid company, military command, etc.).

## Before flying near a protected site

If a protected site may appear in your planned frame:

1. **Identify the site early** during the ground survey.
2. **Check the signage and operator rules** before the flight.
3. **Do not assume** that BGKIS or another airspace approval also covers filming.
4. **Re-plan the shot** or seek site-specific coordination before recording.

## Accidental capture — the correct response

If a protected site enters the frame unexpectedly during a flight:

1. **Redirect the camera** immediately or terminate recording.
2. **Do not publish or share** the footage containing the site.
3. **Isolate the segment** so it is not mixed into the client deliverable.
4. **Follow the site operator's or your compliance contact's instructions** before using, sharing, or deleting the footage.

<Scenario title="You are filming a commercial shoot near an industrial zone. You later notice that the frame briefly captured what appears to be an electrical substation with the prohibition sign. You did not have authorisation. What do you do?">
**Stop, do not publish, isolate, clarify.**

1. Do not distribute the footage to the client until the offending segment is removed.
2. Isolate the affected clip from the working edit and backups used for delivery.
3. Clarify the next step with the site operator or the responsible compliance contact before any publication or reuse.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Assuming BGKIS approval covers filming rights at sensitive sites — it does not.
- Not recognising the prohibition sign on-site before the flight — the pre-flight environment check includes identifying protected facilities in the planned area.
- Sharing accidental footage with the client before removing the protected segment — the prohibition applies to distribution, not only to intentional recording.
- Believing that BGKIS approval automatically solves filming permissions near protected infrastructure.
- Confusing a restricted UGZ zone with a filming prohibition zone — they may overlap but are not identical.
</CommonMistakes>
