## Why GDPR applies from the air

The General Data Protection Regulation (EU 2016/679) applies to any processing of **personal data** — information that relates to an identified or identifiable natural person. From a drone camera, personal data is captured the moment the footage contains:

- **Faces** visible enough to identify a person;
- **Vehicle licence plates** (identifiable property → traceable owner);
- **Voice recordings** if a microphone is active;
- **Behavioural patterns** — filming a person's home over time, revealing when they leave and return.

The pilot's intention is irrelevant. If the data is captured, GDPR governs how it is stored, used, and deleted.

## Lawful basis — required before recording

Article 6 of GDPR requires a lawful basis for processing personal data. For drone pilots the two most relevant bases are:

**Consent (Art. 6(1)(a))** — the person has given clear, specific agreement to be filmed. Verbal consent is weak; written is better. Consent must be freely given — a property owner cannot consent on behalf of passers-by.

**Legitimate interest (Art. 6(1)(f))** — the pilot or their client has a genuine purpose and the privacy impact is proportionate. Examples: a utility company inspecting its own power line infrastructure; a farmer monitoring their own fields. People who happen to appear at the edge of the frame are incidental if they are not identifiable and are not the subject of recording.

**No lawful basis applies** when filming a public park for a commercial video and capturing identifiable people who have not consented. This is the most common violation.

## Practical obligations

Before pressing record near people:
1. Identify whether identifiable persons are likely to enter the frame.
2. Establish the lawful basis in advance — not after the footage exists.
3. If consent is the basis, document it.

After recording:
- **Blur faces and plates** before publishing or sharing footage where individuals are identifiable.
- **Retain footage only as long as the specific purpose requires** — then delete.
- **Do not share footage** with third parties without a separate legal basis.

## Latvia's supervisory authority

In Latvia, GDPR is enforced by the **Datu valsts inspekcija (DVI)**. Complaints trigger investigations. Maximum fines: **€20 million** or **4 % of annual global turnover** — whichever is higher. Individual pilots operating commercially can be fined under the same framework.

<Scenario title="You are filming an aerial video of a public plaza for a city's promotional reel. People are walking below. Some are identifiable. You have no consent from any of them. Is this legal to publish?">
**Not without blurring identifiable individuals.** Filming public spaces for commercial purposes captures personal data. "Public space" does not waive GDPR — it only means the people were visible from outside. The lawful basis for publishing identifiable footage must exist.

Options: obtain a legitimate interest assessment (LIA) showing the privacy impact is minimal, or blur all identifiable faces and plates before publishing. Many production houses use automated blurring tools for exactly this purpose.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Assuming "it's a public space" exempts the pilot from GDPR — it does not.
- Getting verbal consent from a property owner and believing it covers all people who may appear — it does not.
- Storing raw footage indefinitely "just in case" — GDPR requires data minimisation and purpose limitation.
- Publishing footage without blurring faces because "nobody will complain" — the DVI can investigate based on third-party reports, not just direct complaints.
- Confusing airspace permission (UGZ approval) with privacy permission — they are entirely separate legal frameworks.
</CommonMistakes>
