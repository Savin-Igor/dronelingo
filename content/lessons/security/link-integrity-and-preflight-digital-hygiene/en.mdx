## The idea: reduce hidden complexity before takeoff

The aircraft can only be as reliable as the digital chain behind it:

- drone firmware
- controller firmware
- pilot phone or tablet
- flight app
- map cache and airspace data
- SD card and storage settings

If one link is unstable, the whole operation becomes harder to manage under pressure.

## The five most useful habits

**1. Fly with a known device.**  
Do not swap to an unfamiliar tablet at the last moment unless the tested one is unusable. Small UI differences create real delays under stress.

**2. Do not update on mission day unless necessary.**  
An update can fix security issues, but mission day is the wrong moment for an untested software change. Update in a controlled setting, then test.

**3. Refresh maps before going offline.**  
Weak mobile coverage is common at rural sites. If the map and airspace overlays were not refreshed earlier, the pilot loses context exactly where it is needed most.

**4. Confirm home point and RTH logic.**  
A wrong home point turns an automated recovery feature into a risk amplifier.

**5. Reduce app noise.**  
Messaging popups, cloud sync, battery optimisation, and phone calls interrupt attention and sometimes degrade app performance.

## Home point is a safety setting, not a formality

Pilots often treat home-point confirmation as a routine message to tap through. That is a mistake. If takeoff occurred before stable GNSS or if the aircraft was moved after initial lock, return-to-home may aim at the wrong place.

<Scenario title="You arrive at a rural inspection site with weak coverage. The pilot tablet has not refreshed maps since yesterday, one messaging app keeps sending notifications, and the home point message was tapped through quickly. The flight is legal. Is it ready?">
No. The flight may be legally possible, but the digital setup is weak.

The right action is to pause before takeoff:

1. refresh or confirm the required airspace context;
2. close non-essential apps and silence notifications;
3. verify GNSS quality, home point, and RTH altitude;
4. arm only after the digital chain is stable.

This is not bureaucracy. It is risk reduction before the aircraft leaves the ground.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Updating firmware in the car on the way to the mission.
- Flying with an unfamiliar spare phone because “it should be the same.”
- Ignoring stale map tiles and missing overlays in weak coverage areas.
- Accepting the home point without verifying where it actually landed.
- Leaving notifications, calls, and background sync active during flight.
</CommonMistakes>
