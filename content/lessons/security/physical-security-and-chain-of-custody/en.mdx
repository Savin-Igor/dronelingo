## What chain of custody means here

Chain of custody is simply controlled accountability:

- who had the aircraft;
- who had the controller;
- who handled the batteries;
- who handled the storage media;
- when any transfer or handoff happened.

This matters for three reasons:

- **safety** — unknown equipment state is operational risk;
- **security** — unattended gear can be stolen or tampered with;
- **privacy** — media cards and controllers may contain sensitive data.

## The weak moments

Physical security failures usually happen in routine transitions:

- loading or unloading the vehicle;
- battery swaps on the ground;
- client review moments when the pilot hands over the screen;
- post-incident confusion;
- rushed pack-down at the end of the day.

The fix is not paranoia. The fix is simple discipline.

## Media handling matters

An SD card is small, easy to misplace, and often more valuable than the drone because it contains:

- client work,
- evidence after an incident,
- personal data,
- potentially sensitive site imagery.

Loose cards in jacket pockets or mixed accessory bags are poor practice.

## Borrowed equipment is a trust boundary

If batteries, payloads, or aircraft move between people or organisations, label and document them. Unknown history creates unknown risk.

<Scenario title="During a busy field day, the pilot leaves the controller on a table while walking 30 metres away to move cones. A client is nearby, spare batteries are open, and one SD card is loose beside the case. Why is this more serious than it looks?">
Because several control points were lost at once.

The unattended controller can be tampered with or taken. The open batteries can be mixed. The loose SD card can be lost or copied. None of these require a malicious scene to become a problem; ordinary confusion is enough.

The correct habit is to keep the controller in controlled hands, batteries labelled and separated, and media stored immediately after use.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Leaving controllers or open cases unattended “just for a minute.”
- Mixing used and fresh batteries without clear labels.
- Carrying SD cards loose in pockets or unmarked pouches.
- Handing devices to clients without controlling what else is accessible on them.
</CommonMistakes>
