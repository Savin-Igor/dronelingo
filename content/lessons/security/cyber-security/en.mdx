# UAS cyber security

<MissionBriefing objective="After this lesson you will identify the four main cyber attack vectors against a UAS, recognise the difference between GNSS jamming and spoofing, and apply basic security hygiene to your aircraft, app, and data.">
A drone is a networked computer that also flies. The attack surface is the pilot's phone, the manufacturer's cloud, and the GPS signals the drone trusts unconditionally. Each is exploitable.

## The four attack vectors

A UAS system consists of four components, each with its own exposure:

**1. The pilot app and account.** Manufacturer apps (DJI GO 4, DJI Fly, Autel Sky) are linked to accounts that contain flight logs, location history, and settings. Weak passwords, unrotated credentials, and reused passwords across services are the primary attack vector here.

**2. Firmware and software update channels.** A drone's autopilot, camera, and remote controller run firmware. Malicious firmware injected via fake update servers can disable safety features, alter flight behaviour, or exfiltrate data. Only update via the **official manufacturer channel** — never from third-party sites, even for "unlocking" regional restrictions.

**3. Radio link and GPS signals.** The control link (typically 2.4/5.8 GHz) and video downlink are radio signals. Public Wi-Fi hotspots near the operation can interfere. The GPS signals the drone uses for positioning are unencrypted civilian signals — vulnerable to both jamming and spoofing.

**4. Local storage and cloud.** Video recorded to SD cards or uploaded to manufacturer clouds is personal data if it contains identifiable people. Unencrypted SD cards can be read by anyone who handles the drone. Cloud accounts without 2FA are single-password protected.

## GNSS jamming vs. spoofing

**Jamming** is the simpler attack: an RF noise source overwhelms the GPS signal band, causing the drone to lose satellite lock. Symptoms: sudden "grey map" in the app, GPS accuracy warnings, RTH failure. The drone does not know where it is. **Immediate action: switch to manual mode, use visual references, land safely.**

**Spoofing** is more sophisticated: a transmitter sends fake GPS signals that override the legitimate ones. The drone believes it is flying at a different location or altitude. Symptoms: position jumps to a geographically implausible location (kilometres away), unexpected route deviations, altitude anomalies. **Immediate action: same — manual mode, visual references, land. Do not trust any app-displayed position.**

Both are increasing in frequency in Latvia, particularly near the eastern border (NATO operational area), and have been documented at Riga city centre. They are not hypothetical.

## Basic security hygiene — five rules

1. **Change the factory password** on the manufacturer app account immediately after registering.
2. **Enable 2FA** on all accounts linked to the UAS (manufacturer app, cloud storage, email recovery).
3. **Update firmware only from official channels** — manufacturer website or official app update mechanism only.
4. **Never connect the UAS or controller to public or unknown Wi-Fi** — use a personal hotspot or LTE connection.
5. **Encrypt sensitive SD card footage** if the content is operationally sensitive (inspection data, client materials).

## Data privacy in the UAS

The SD card and cloud account hold footage that may be:
- Personal data under GDPR (identifiable people in frame).
- Commercially sensitive (client site details, proprietary infrastructure).
- Security-relevant (critical infrastructure captured accidentally).

Retention rule: delete footage that has no current purpose. Do not accumulate gigabytes of unfiled footage on an unencrypted card.

<Scenario title="Mid-flight over Riga city centre, your drone's displayed position suddenly jumps 4 km to a location over the sea. The drone's actual position is still visually confirmed in front of you at the park. What is happening and what do you do?">
This is the classic symptom of **GNSS spoofing**. The drone's GPS receiver is accepting fake signals that override its true position.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Do not rely on the app map for position, altitude, or RTH — all are compromised.
2. Switch to **manual / attitude mode** immediately.
3. Maintain VLOS and use visual references to land at the nearest safe spot.
4. After landing: screenshot the app showing the position anomaly, note the time and location.
5. File a report with CAA Latvia — GNSS incidents in controlled airspace are mandatory to report.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Using the manufacturer app on the same phone that connects to public Wi-Fi routinely — the device becomes an attack vector.
- Trusting third-party firmware "unlock" tools — they can introduce backdoors and void manufacturer support.
- Not recognising spoofing because "the app shows a location, it must be working" — a spoofed position is an actively misleading position.
- Storing months of client footage on unencrypted SD cards in the drone bag — physical theft of the bag exposes all data.
- Not reporting GNSS anomalies to CAA Latvia — the pattern across multiple reports is what allows authorities to identify active jamming/spoofing sources.
</CommonMistakes>
