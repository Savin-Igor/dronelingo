# Occurrence reporting

Civil aviation learns from near-misses, not only accidents. Every event filed is a data point that prevents the next one. The pilot who reports a close call is not admitting fault — they are contributing to a system that protects everyone.

## Two reporting categories

**Mandatory reporting** — required by law within 72 hours of the event. Missing the deadline is a separate offence from the underlying incident.

**Voluntary reporting** — no deadline, no obligation, but strongly encouraged for any safety-related event that did not meet the mandatory threshold.

## Mandatory reports — three triggers

A pilot **must** file a mandatory report within 72 hours when any of the following occurs:

1. **Serious injury or fatality** — the UAS causes bodily harm to a third party requiring medical treatment.
2. **Near-miss or collision with a manned aircraft** — any situation where a manned aircraft had to manoeuvre to avoid the UAS, or where separation was insufficient by the crew's assessment.
3. **Strike on critical infrastructure** — the UAS physically contacts a power line, railway, communications mast, or other protected infrastructure.

These are not discretionary. "Near-miss" is interpreted broadly — if a manned aircraft crew filed an AIRPROX report, the UAS pilot should assume a mandatory report is required.

## Voluntary reports — the wider net

Voluntary reports are encouraged for any event with safety implications that did not meet the mandatory threshold:

- Lost data link during flight (link recovered, safe landing achieved).
- Fly-away event where control was regained before harm occurred.
- Fail-safe did not activate as configured.
- GNSS jamming or spoofing detected during flight.
- Hardware failure discovered during flight (motor warning, battery cell failure).
- Geofence anomaly or erroneous geo-awareness data.

There is no penalty for filing voluntarily. There is a systemic cost to not filing — these events never enter the safety database, the pattern is never detected, and the next pilot faces the same unwarned hazard.

## Where to file

**Latvia:** `e.caa.gov.lv/incidents` — the primary portal for both mandatory and voluntary reports. Available in Latvian and English.

**EU-wide:** `aviationreporting.eu` — the ECCAIRS 2 system that aggregates reports across all EU member states. Relevant for events involving cross-border operations or shared airspace.

Reports should include: date, time, location (GPS if available), UAS type, what occurred, what actions were taken, and any witness or bystander contact details.

## Just Culture — the protection that makes reporting work

EU Regulation 376/2014 establishes **Just Culture** as a legal principle in civil aviation reporting. The core guarantee:

- Filing a report **does not automatically trigger criminal proceedings** against the reporter.
- Authorities are required to separate the safety investigation from any enforcement action.
- Criminal consequences apply only when the event resulted from **gross negligence** or **deliberate misconduct**.

In practice: a pilot who loses link, lands safely, and files a voluntary report is protected from prosecution for the incident. A pilot who does not report a mandatory event — a near-miss with a manned aircraft — has no protection and may face charges for the omission itself.

**Silence is not safer.** The omission of a mandatory report is a separately prosecutable act.

<Scenario title="Your drone loses GPS and drifts 80 m downwind before you regain manual control and land safely. No one was hurt. No aircraft were nearby. Is this a mandatory or voluntary report?">
**Voluntary.** The event does not meet any mandatory trigger — no injury, no manned aircraft involved, no critical infrastructure struck. However, it is a clear candidate for a voluntary report:

- Lost GPS position control during flight is a safety-relevant event.
- The drift distance and wind conditions are useful data for the safety authority.
- If the same GPS failure pattern appears in other reports, CAA Latvia can notify the manufacturer.

File at `e.caa.gov.lv/incidents`. Takes approximately 10 minutes. The Just Culture protection applies to voluntary reports — there is no downside to filing.

<CommonMistakes>
- Waiting more than 72 hours to file a mandatory report because "I wanted to think about it" — the deadline is 72 hours from the event, not from when the pilot decides to file.
- Assuming a near-miss only counts if the manned aircraft filed a report first — the UAS pilot's reporting obligation is independent.
- Not filing voluntary reports for events that resolved safely — "nothing happened" misses the point; the near-miss data is the value.
- Believing Just Culture means no consequences ever — gross negligence and deliberate misconduct are explicitly not protected.
- Filing at the wrong portal — EU-wide `aviationreporting.eu` does not replace the mandatory Latvian report at `e.caa.gov.lv/incidents`.
</CommonMistakes>
