# Batteries and storage

## LiPo fundamentals

Lithium Polymer (LiPo) cells operate between **3.0 V** (depleted — do not discharge below this) and **4.2 V** (fully charged) per cell. The nominal voltage is **3.7 V** per cell.

Energy content is calculated as: **Wh = mAh ÷ 1000 × nominal voltage × cell count**. A 4S 5000 mAh pack: 5 Ah × 14.8 V ≈ **74 Wh** — relevant for air travel limits.

The **C rating** tells you the maximum safe discharge rate as a multiple of capacity. A 5000 mAh battery rated at 20C can safely deliver up to 100 A continuous.

## Storage charge — the rule most pilots skip

A fully charged LiPo sitting unused generates heat internally and degrades faster than one stored at the correct level.

| Storage period | Target charge |
|----------------|---------------|
| < 3 days (flying again soon) | 100 % |
| 3–14 days | 70–80 % |
| **> 2 weeks** | **50–60 % (storage charge)** |

Most modern drone apps have a "storage discharge" mode that brings cells to the correct level automatically. Use it — do not leave batteries at full charge in a bag for months.

Store batteries in a **Li-Po safe bag** (fireproof ceramic-fibre pouch) in a cool, dry location. Not in the car boot in summer.

## Signs a battery must not fly

A **swollen** battery has gas building inside the cell — a sign of internal damage or overcharge. This is a fire risk under the mechanical stress of flight vibration.

Check before every flight:
- Is the pack visibly puffed or asymmetric?
- Are any cells below 3.0 V after storage (over-discharged)?
- Does the outer casing have cracks or damage?

**Any "yes" = do not fly.** Dispose of swollen cells at certified collection points (in Latvia: partner sites listed at `dabasdati.lv`). Do not put them in household waste.

## Air travel limits (IATA)

Drone batteries fall under IATA Dangerous Goods regulations for lithium ion cells:

| Wh rating | Allowed in aircraft |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **< 100 Wh** | Carry-on or checked baggage |
| **100–160 Wh** | Carry-on only, with **airline written approval** |
| **> 160 Wh** | Forbidden in passenger aircraft cabin |

Terminals at some airports will ask for the Wh figure — calculate it from the label before departure. Do not charge batteries on the aircraft.

<Scenario title="You arrive at a job site and find that the only charged battery you brought is visibly puffed at one corner. The client is waiting. What do you do?">
**Do not fly.** A puffed battery under the vibration and current draw of a flight can rupture and ignite. The consequences — a burning drone over a client's site — are worse than a postponed shoot.

The professional response: inform the client, reschedule, and dispose of the battery correctly. Most commercial photographers carry two sets of batteries. This situation is an argument for redundancy, not a reason to fly with a defective cell.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Storing batteries at 100 % charge between flights — degrades cell chemistry and increases fire risk over weeks.
- Not checking for swelling before flight — a puffed pack is not cosmetic; it is a structural failure waiting to happen.
- Calculating Wh incorrectly for air travel — forgetting to account for cell count (S rating) leads to underestimating capacity.
- Leaving batteries in a hot car boot — temperatures above 60 °C accelerate degradation and can trigger thermal runaway.
- Disposing of LiPo cells in household waste — lithium fires in waste facilities are a documented hazard; use certified collection.
</CommonMistakes>
