## Three different functions

The aircraft uses several navigation references:

- **GNSS** gives position;
- **compass** gives heading orientation;
- **home point** stores the return reference location.

These functions interact, but they are not the same.

If GNSS is wrong, the aircraft may think it is somewhere else.  
If the compass is wrong, the aircraft may know where it is but misjudge which way it is pointing.  
If the home point is wrong, return-to-home may guide the aircraft to the wrong place even when position is otherwise good.

## Why home point deserves a separate check

Pilots sometimes assume home point is correct because the app showed a message once. That is weak practice.

You should verify:

- that GNSS quality was stable when it was set;
- that the aircraft was not moved after the initial lock in a way that changes the intended recovery point;
- that the stored point makes sense for the actual operating area.

## Compass problems are not the same as GNSS problems

Typical compass issues include:

- incorrect heading display,
- yaw instability or drift,
- odd orientation behaviour after takeoff.

Typical GNSS issues include:

- poor position hold,
- location jumps,
- weak or lost satellite lock.

Calling both “GPS trouble” hides the real problem.

## Why this matters to automation

Features like position hold and return-to-home depend on correct inputs. Pilots who do not understand the difference between those inputs are more likely to trust automation when it is least trustworthy.

<Scenario title="You take off from an open site. The app confirms home point, but the aircraft had been powered while still beside a vehicle and then moved several metres before actual launch. The aircraft later needs to recover automatically. What is the risk?">
The risk is that the stored home point may not match the best actual recovery location.

Even if GNSS later looks normal, return-to-home may aim for a less safe or simply wrong reference position. That is why home-point confirmation is not a tap-through message; it is a navigation safety check.
</Scenario>

</CommonMistakes>
