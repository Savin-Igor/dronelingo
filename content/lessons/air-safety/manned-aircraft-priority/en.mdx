## Why the rule is strict

In the Open category, the remote pilot must avoid creating conflicts with other airspace users. In practice, that means a UAS must give way to manned aircraft.

The reason is not symbolic. A helicopter or light aircraft:

- moves faster than your UAS
- may be task-loaded or flying low for a reason you cannot see
- may not detect a small drone early enough to avoid it safely

The pilot who assumes “they will see me” is building the whole safety case on the other aircraft’s luck.

## What not to do

When a manned aircraft appears, the wrong instincts are common:

- climbing higher “to be seen”
- flying closer to identify it
- finishing the shot first
- staring at the map instead of flying visually

All four increase risk. The priority is not curiosity or footage. The priority is reducing the number of variables in the air.

## The practical decision

If you can maintain obvious separation and safely continue, do so conservatively. If there is any doubt, land.

This is one of those moments where a decisive landing is more professional than a technically arguable continuation.

<Scenario title="You are flying at 55 m AGL over open ground. You hear a helicopter before you can identify its direction. Your observer points left and says it is approaching low, but still far away. What is the correct response?">
Immediately stop the task, bring the drone lower, move it away from the likely path if needed, and be ready to land at once. If the approach path is uncertain, land without delay.

The key is not to “win” the separation judgment. The key is to reduce conflict while the situation is still easy.
</Scenario>
