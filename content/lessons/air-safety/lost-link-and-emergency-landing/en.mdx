## What “lost link” means in practice

The problem may affect one or more parts of the control chain:

- **control link** — the aircraft responds late or not at all;
- **video link** — the image freezes or breaks up while the aircraft may still be controllable;
- **telemetry trust** — values update incorrectly, too slowly, or implausibly.

The pilot's task is not to diagnose everything perfectly in the air. The task is to decide whether the aircraft remains safely manageable.

## The right priorities

When the link starts to fail, priorities narrow fast:

1. **fly the aircraft first**;
2. **stop all non-essential camera or mission tasks**;
3. **bring the aircraft closer and simpler**;
4. **use the pre-identified landing area**;
5. **land before the situation gets worse**.

The common mistake is waiting for certainty that the failure is “real.” By the time certainty arrives, options are usually worse.

## When return-to-home helps — and when it does not

Return-to-home can be useful if:

- the home point is correct;
- GNSS is trustworthy;
- the route back is obstacle-safe;
- the aircraft is still behaving normally.

It is a poor default if location data is suspect, obstacles are nearby, or the aircraft response is already erratic. Automation is only as good as the data it is using.

## Emergency landing is a professional action

Pilots sometimes treat emergency landing as failure. It is not. It is controlled risk reduction.

<Scenario title="You are filming near a treeline. The live image freezes twice, then returns. Telemetry still shows position and battery, but stick response feels slightly delayed. What is the correct reaction?">
Treat the event as degraded and potentially worsening.

The right action is to stop filming, reduce range, avoid the trees, orient toward the safest landing area, and land promptly. Do not continue the shot just because the image returned. The recovery of one channel does not prove the whole link is stable.
</Scenario>
