# Visual line of sight (VLOS)

In the Open category, the remote pilot must keep the unmanned aircraft in
**visual line of sight (VLOS)** at all times.

## What VLOS means

The pilot sees the aircraft unaided. Prescription glasses or contact lenses
are allowed, but binoculars and zoomed cameras are not. The pilot must be
able to detect the aircraft's **attitude and direction** of travel — not
merely confirm a dot in the sky.

How far that is in practice depends on the size of the aircraft and the
weather. A small drone may be invisible at 200 m on a hazy day; a larger one
may be tracked at 500 m on a clear one.

## First-person view (FPV) is not VLOS

When the pilot looks at goggles or a phone screen instead of the aircraft,
they are flying **FPV** — and that breaks VLOS. FPV is permitted in the Open
category **only if a separate UAS observer stands next to the pilot** and
keeps the airspace under direct watch.

The observer must be:
- physically next to the pilot (no radio, no phone link)
- watching the airspace, not the screen

## Why VLOS matters

The whole low-risk premise of the Open category rests on the pilot being
able to see and react to other airspace users. The moment that link is
broken, the operation drops outside the category — and outside the
remote pilot's authority.

> **Source:** Reg (EU) 2019/947, UAS.OPEN.060(3); EASA Easy Access Rules
> for UAS (consolidated 2024-07).
