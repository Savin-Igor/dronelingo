# Visual line of sight (VLOS)

<MissionBriefing objective="After this lesson you will know what VLOS is, how it differs from FPV, and the only legal way to fly FPV in the Open category.">
VLOS is the foundation of every Open-category flight. The whole "low risk"
premise of the category collapses the moment you stop seeing the aircraft.
</MissionBriefing>

## What VLOS means

The pilot sees the aircraft **unaided**. Prescription glasses or contact
lenses are allowed. Binoculars and zoomed cameras are not. The pilot must
be able to detect the aircraft's **attitude and direction** — not merely
confirm a dot in the sky.

How far that is in practice depends on the size of the aircraft and the
weather. A small drone may be invisible at 200 m on a hazy day; a larger
one may be tracked at 500 m on a clear one. There is no fixed metre value
for VLOS distance — there is only "can the pilot still tell which way it's
pointing?"

## First-person view (FPV) is not VLOS

When the pilot looks at goggles or a phone screen instead of the aircraft,
they are flying **FPV** — and that breaks VLOS. FPV is permitted in the
Open category only if a separate **UAS observer** stands next to the pilot
and keeps the airspace under direct watch.

The observer must be:

- physically next to the pilot (no radio, no phone link)
- watching the airspace, not the screen

<Scenario title="You're flying C1 with FPV goggles. Your friend wants to spot for you from 100 m away over a walkie-talkie.">
That is **not legal** in the Open category. The observer-FPV rule requires
direct, unmediated voice contact: the observer must stand next to you. A
radio link is not "next to." Either land, or have your friend walk over.
</Scenario>

<CommonMistakes>
- Treating "I can hear it" or "I can see it on the screen" as VLOS — neither counts.
- Using a radio between pilot and observer. Direct voice only.
- Trusting binoculars or zoom to recover VLOS on a drone that's too far. Once it's a dot, you're outside VLOS regardless of magnification.
</CommonMistakes>
