The full text of **Reg (EU) 2019/947** plus its sister **2019/945** and the EASA Easy Access Rules runs to ~600 pages. The vast majority of that text never touches a hobby pilot. Here is what you actually need to know — in plain English.

## The three-category structure

The whole regulation hangs off one decision: how risky is your operation?

| Category | Who it is for | Authorisation |
|---|---|---|
| **Open** | Hobby + low-risk commercial | None (just registration + certificate) |
| **Specific** | Higher-risk commercial (BVLOS, populated areas, heavy drones) | Operational authorisation or PDRA / SORA |
| **Certified** | Aircraft-grade ops (passenger drones, large cargo) | Full type certification, like a manned aircraft |

If you are reading this, you are almost certainly in **Open**. The rest of this article focuses there.

## Open category — the rules that bind you

Five rules cover 90% of Open operations:

1. **Drone under 25 kg MTOM**
2. **Visual Line Of Sight (VLOS)** at all times
3. **Maximum 120 m AGL** (above ground level)
4. **Stay out of crowds**, regardless of distance
5. **Do not drop dangerous goods**

Inside Open, three subcategories — A1, A2, A3 — refine these rules ([detailed comparison](/blog/eu-open-category-a1-a2-a3-explained)). All three obey the five rules above.

## VLOS — the rule that's misunderstood most

VLOS means **the remote pilot can see the drone with unaided vision** at all times. Three points trip pilots up:

- **Unaided** means no binoculars, no FPV goggles, no zoom. You see the drone with your eyes.
- **Continuous** means *not* "I glance at it occasionally." It is a real-time visual link, broken only briefly to consult the controller.
- **The drone itself** — not its position on a map. You see the airframe.

FPV (first-person-view) flight is allowed in Open **only with a dedicated visual observer** standing next to the pilot who maintains VLOS continuously.

## 120 m AGL — and where it doesn't apply

The 120 m height cap is measured **from the ground directly below the drone**, not from take-off point. Two implications:

- Flying over a 100 m hill at "120 m AGL" means you can climb to 220 m above your take-off point
- But near tall obstacles, you may exceed 120 m AGL with permission — for inspections of cell towers and chimneys taller than 105 m, you can climb up to **15 m above the obstacle** even if that puts you above 120 m absolute

The 120 m limit is also overridden by airport CTRs (drone flight forbidden regardless of height) and by airspace classes G/E above 120 m which become controlled airspace.

## Operator registration

Every operator (the person responsible) registers with their national CAA. In Latvia, this is at [e.caa.gov.lv](/blog/drone-registration-latvia-step-by-step). One registration covers all your drones. The CAA issues an **operator number**, which you mark on every drone you own and enter into the drone's Remote ID broadcaster.

## Theoretical knowledge

For A1/A3, take a 40-question online exam at e.caa.gov.lv. For A2, an additional 30-question exam plus a self-declaration of practical training. We cover the exam in detail [here](/blog/drone-exam-latvia-what-to-expect).

## C-classes and what they mean

After January 2024, EU drones are sold with a **C-class** label (C0 through C6) determined by weight, max speed, kinetic energy, and built-in safety features (geo-awareness, Remote ID, low-speed mode). The C-class determines which subcategory you can fly under:

| C-class | Mass | Subcategory eligibility |
|---|---|---|
| C0 | < 250 g | A1 |
| C1 | < 900 g | A1 |
| C2 | < 4 kg | A2 (and A3) |
| C3 | < 25 kg | A3 |
| C4 | < 25 kg | A3 (FPV/competition variant) |
| C5/C6 | n/a | Specific category |

Drones manufactured before 2024 without a C-class fall under **legacy rules** — broadly mapping to C-classes but with one major restriction: from January 2024, legacy drones above 250 g without Remote ID are restricted to A3 only.

## Where the regulation gets weird

Three areas where Reg 2019/947 surprises pilots:

1. **Geographical zones** — member states (including Latvia) can define their own no-fly zones on top of the regulation. The EU does not centrally publish them. You verify locally ([Latvia map and tools](/blog/no-fly-zones-latvia)).

2. **U-Space** — a parallel regulatory layer for automated airspace management is rolling out. As of 2026, the EU has approved U-Space but most member states have not designated U-Space zones yet. When they do, BVLOS operations in those zones will require additional service-provider integration.

3. **Insurance** — required for commercial operations under Reg 785/2004, declared at registration ([detail](/blog/drone-insurance-eu-required-or-recommended)).
