Reg. (EU) `2019/947` is the operational backbone of drone flying in Europe. You do not need to memorise the whole legal text, but you do need to understand the structure it creates.

## The three categories

The regulation divides drone operations into three categories:

| Category | Main idea |
|---|---|
| **Open** | lower-risk operations inside fixed limits |
| **Specific** | operations that go beyond those fixed limits |
| **Certified** | the highest-risk operations, closer to manned-aviation logic |

For most private pilots and many simple commercial flights, the relevant category is **Open**.

## What Open is built on

The Latvian CAA Open-category summary highlights the core limits that shape day-to-day flying:

- aircraft up to `25 kg`
- flight in **VLOS**
- maximum height of `120 m`
- clear limits around uninvolved people and crowds
- compliance with geographical-zone restrictions

The Open category is then split into **A1, A2, and A3**, depending on the aircraft and the distance to uninvolved people.

## Registration is part of the system

The regulation does not start with the aircraft alone. It also identifies the **operator** and the **remote pilot** as separate roles.

In Latvia, that distinction matters in practice because:

- the **operator** receives the number used for drone marking
- the **remote pilot** is the person linked to the qualification path

If you fly as a private person in your own name, you usually hold both roles at once.

## Qualification depends on the operation

One of the most common mistakes is to talk about “a drone licence” as if there were only one.

Under the Open-category logic:

- **A1/A3** is the basic qualification path where required
- **A2** is an additional step for the closer `C2` case

That is why qualification questions should always be read together with:

- aircraft class or mass
- subcategory
- distance from uninvolved people

## C-class labels matter

Reg. (EU) `2019/945` sits next to `2019/947` and defines the class-marking system used in practice:

- `C0`
- `C1`
- `C2`
- `C3`
- `C4`

Those labels are not just technical stickers. They affect which Open-category subcategory the aircraft can realistically fit into.

## Geographical zones are national

The EU regulation allows member states to define their own **UAS geographical zones**. That is why one of the most important local checks in Latvia is not in the EU text itself, but in the national zone system and the official Latvian map.

So the practical reading order is:

1. understand the EU category and subcategory
2. confirm the aircraft class or mass
3. then apply the Latvian geographical-zone restrictions

## Legacy aircraft still need careful reading

Older aircraft without a C-class mark are not automatically unusable, but they do not fit the system as cleanly as newer class-marked aircraft. The Latvian CAA summary makes clear that, after the transition period, legacy aircraft often end up with narrower Open-category options.

That means older drones should be checked case by case against:

- aircraft mass
- intended subcategory
- local operating environment

## What this regulation is really doing

At a practical level, Reg. (EU) `2019/947` is doing one thing: it translates risk into rules you can apply before take-off.

Instead of asking “Can this drone fly?”, the better question is:

- in which **category** is the operation?
- in which **subcategory** does it fit?
- what **registration**, **qualification**, and **local restrictions** follow from that?

If those answers are clear, the rest of the regulation becomes much easier to read.
