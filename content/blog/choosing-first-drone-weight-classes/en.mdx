Most people shopping for a first drone start with camera quality and flight time. From a regulatory point of view, the more useful starting point is **class and mass**.

That is what decides:

- where the aircraft can realistically be used
- whether qualification is needed
- how restrictive the operation becomes

## Why class matters more than marketing

In the EU system, the aircraft class is not decoration. It affects the Open-category path available to you.

A simple first-pass view is:

| Aircraft type | What it usually means for a beginner |
|---|---|
| `<250 g` / `C0` | simplest entry point, but still not “rule-free” |
| `C1` | still flexible, but with clearer limits around uninvolved people |
| `C2` | closer-to-people work only with the additional A2 path |
| `C3` / `C4` | usually a more restrictive Open-category use case |

## Under 250 g is simpler, not lawless

The biggest misunderstanding is that a very light drone means “no real rules”.

That is not true.

A lighter aircraft can reduce the qualification burden, but you still need to check:

- operator registration
- geographical zones
- privacy and data-protection implications
- the actual Open-category limits for the operation

So a first drone under `250 g` is often the easiest place to start, but it is not a legal shortcut around the framework.

## C1 is the next step up

Once you move into the `C1` range, you usually gain aircraft capability, but you also make the rule set more demanding.

So a first-time buyer should not assume “bigger is safer”. In regulatory terms, a heavier aircraft often means:

- less operational flexibility
- more care around uninvolved people
- a higher chance that the intended use case no longer fits the simplest path

## C2 changes the qualification question

If the aircraft is `C2`, the purchase decision is no longer just about hardware. It directly raises the qualification question, because the closer-to-people use case is tied to the **A2** path.

So before buying a `C2` aircraft as a first drone, the practical question is:

- do you actually need that operational profile now?

If the answer is no, a lighter class is often the cleaner first step.

## Legacy aircraft deserve extra care

Older drones without a C-class mark can still be perfectly usable, but they need closer legal reading than newer class-marked aircraft.

Before buying a used drone, check:

- the aircraft mass
- whether it has a class mark
- which Open-category use case you want

That matters more than cosmetic condition or sales talk.

## A better buying question

Instead of asking “Which drone is best?”, ask:

1. Where do I realistically want to fly?
2. How close to people do I need to operate?
3. Do I want the simplest qualification path, or am I ready for a stricter one?
4. Will a legacy aircraft create more restrictions than I want?

For many beginners, that points toward the lightest aircraft that still covers the real use case.
