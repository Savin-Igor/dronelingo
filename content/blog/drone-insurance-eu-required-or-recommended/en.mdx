**Civil-liability insurance** is mandatory for any commercial drone flight in the EU and required at operator registration when commercial intent is declared. For pure hobby use it is not legally required for sub-20 kg drones — but a single mid-air collision with a person, car, or window can produce a five-figure claim that you will pay out of pocket.

This guide walks through what the regulation actually says, where the EU-level requirement comes from, and how to choose a policy that fits.

## What the regulation says

Two regulations govern this:

- **Reg (EC) 785/2004** is the EU-wide source. It mandates minimum civil-liability insurance for **commercial** aircraft operators, with the minimum cover scaled by maximum take-off mass (MTOM).
- **Reg (EU) 2019/947** Art. 14 requires operators to **declare** insurance details at registration. The CAA itself does not check the policy line-by-line, but a declaration of false data is grounds for revocation of operator status and a criminal complaint.

For drones with MTOM under 500 kg (i.e., every Open and most Specific drones), the EU minimum is **750 000 SDR** in liability cover — roughly €930 000 at recent exchange rates.

## Commercial vs hobby — where the line sits

"Commercial" in this context is broader than you might expect. You are commercial if:

- You take any payment, even one-off, for drone footage or services
- You publish drone footage on a monetised channel (YouTube AdSense, Instagram with brand deals, TikTok Creator Fund)
- You fly for your employer's purposes — even unpaid — including a hospital documenting its rooftop or a contractor inspecting their own client's site

You are hobby if:

- You fly purely for personal enjoyment
- You share footage on private social channels without monetisation
- You give footage to friends or family without payment

The CAA reads "commercial" wide. If you are not sure which side of the line you sit on, treat yourself as commercial.

## What a typical policy covers

A drone civil-liability policy typically covers:

- **Third-party bodily injury** — someone struck or distressed by your drone
- **Third-party property damage** — windows, vehicles, livestock
- **Legal defence costs** — including reasonable solicitor fees

It does **not** cover:

- Damage to your own drone (separate hull insurance, optional)
- Loss of footage / commercial losses
- Intentional misuse (flying inside a CTR knowingly, etc.)
- Damage caused by an uninsured pilot operating under your operator number

## What it costs in 2026

For a hobby pilot with a sub-4 kg drone, annual civil-liability policies in Latvia start around **€30–60/year** from BTA, Balta, Gjensidige, or pan-EU specialists like Coverdrone (UK-based but covering EU operations). Commercial cover scales with declared exposure and starts around **€150/year**.

A typical Open-category hobby policy:

- **€1,000,000 third-party liability**
- **€100–200 hull cover** option (cheaper to self-insure)
- **€30/year** premium
- No deductible on liability; €100 deductible on hull

Compare apples-to-apples by looking at the **liability limit**, **geographic scope** (EU vs. EEA vs. worldwide), and **excluded operations** (e.g., night flight, BVLOS, agricultural spraying).

## What CAA Latvia actually asks for

At operator registration ([guide](/blog/drone-registration-latvia-step-by-step)), you supply:

- **Policy number**
- **Insurer name**
- **Validity dates**
- **Liability limit**

CAA does not verify the policy against the insurer's database. The declaration is on your honour and is subject to inspection during incidents.

## Common mistakes

1. **Assuming household insurance covers it.** Most household policies explicitly exclude aircraft. Confirm in writing before relying on it.

2. **Buying a "drone insurance" that is only hull cover.** Hull-only does not satisfy CAA's liability declaration. Confirm liability is the primary cover.

3. **Letting the policy lapse without informing CAA.** Operating without the declared insurance is grounds for criminal charges in commercial flight. Renew or update before the policy expires.
