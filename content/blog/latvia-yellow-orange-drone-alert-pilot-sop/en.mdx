On 23 May 2026, Latvia's National Armed Forces (NBS) switched on a two-tier cell-broadcast alert system for airspace threats. No app required, no subscription, no opt-in — the message arrives on every phone connected to any Latvian carrier, in whatever language the device is set to.

The system has two levels: **Yellow** and **Orange**. Each carries a different meaning and a different expected response. If you fly a drone in Latvia, understanding that difference before you need it is not optional.

## What Yellow and Orange actually mean

**Yellow** is an informational alert. NBS sends it when there is a *possible* threat in Latvian airspace. It does not require you to stop what you are doing. It is a signal that the situation has changed and warrants attention.

**Orange** is an action alert. NBS sends it when a threat has been *detected* — not suspected, detected. On iOS devices, Orange arrives under the label "Extreme Alert." The instructions inside the message are specific and require an immediate response.

The level can shift in either direction. NBS may downgrade from Orange back to Yellow as a situation develops, or escalate from Yellow to Orange. When the threat is over, NBS sends an explicit end-of-alert broadcast. Additional updates appear on the **"Latvijas armija"** social media account.

## What the Orange message tells civilians

The instructions inside an Orange alert address the general public. They are:

- Go indoors and seek a safe place.
- Follow the **two-wall principle**: put at least two solid walls between yourself and the outside.
- If you see a low-flying suspicious object, **do not approach it**. Call **112**.
- Wait for an NBS all-clear before resuming normal activity.

Reference resources for more context: **sargs.lv** and **112.lv**.

Col. Arvis Zīle, head of the NBS Crisis Management Center, has previously noted that Latvia already operates up to six distinct cell-broadcast message types. That context matters: if your phone buzzes, it pays to read the message rather than assume the category.

## Our reading for drone pilots

NBS guidance is written for the general public; it does not address drone pilots specifically. For pilots, we read it as follows — this is our interpretation, not an NBS instruction.

**Yellow received while flying:**

- Heightened awareness. Finish your current task, but prioritize a safe and direct landing path.
- Be ready to abort at short notice. The operating environment has changed even if no legal obligation to land has kicked in yet.
- Start scanning for a suitable landing spot now, while you still have time.

**Orange received while flying:**

1. Initiate landing or Return to Home immediately.
2. Note the time and your aircraft's GPS coordinates before landing.
3. Once down, shut down the aircraft.
4. Follow the civilian instructions: move indoors, apply the two-wall principle.
5. Do not approach any unknown low-flying object. Call 112 if you observe one.
6. Wait for the NBS all-clear before powering on again.

**After either level — log it.** Record which alert level you received, the time, and what action you took. If your operation later draws a CAA Latvia review, a timestamped log entry is straightforward evidence that you responded proportionately.

## What this changes about pre-flight planning

The cell-broadcast system works without internet and without an app, but it depends on cellular coverage. If you fly in areas with weak or no cell signal, you may not receive an alert.

Three things worth adding to your pre-flight routine:

- **Check cell signal at your launch site.** If coverage is marginal, weigh that risk explicitly before takeoff.
- **Set your device language to one you read quickly under pressure.** The message language follows the device locale. A phone set to a language you do not read fluently will slow your response.
- **Test that Extreme Alerts are enabled.** On most devices, Extreme Alerts can be disabled in system settings. Verify the setting before you fly.

None of this replaces airspace authorization or NOTAM checks. It is an additional layer that sits on top of your normal pre-flight checks.

## Why the system exists now

Latvia recorded a series of unauthorized drone incursions into its airspace during May 2026. The two-tier cell-broadcast system is one part of the national response. The system is threat-agnostic by design — it covers any detected airspace threat, not a specific type or origin.

The Yellow/Orange structure is specifically designed to address a problem Col. Zīle identified: Latvia already uses multiple cell-broadcast categories, and a single undifferentiated alert creates confusion about urgency. Two clearly labeled levels with different expected responses reduce that ambiguity.

## What you should do today

The system is already live. You do not need to register anything or download anything. What you do need is a clear mental model of the two levels and a practiced response for each.

Yellow: heightened awareness, plan your exit, stay ready.
Orange: land now, document, go indoors, wait for all-clear.
