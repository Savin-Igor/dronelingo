If you want to legally fly a drone weighing more than 250 g in Latvia — or any drone with a camera, no matter how light — you need the **A1/A3 Open-category certificate**. The good news: the process is entirely online, free of charge, and can be done in a single afternoon. The less good news: the official 40-question theory exam is harder than people expect, with a 75 % pass mark and a question bank that bites into the parts of EU drone regulation most pilots skim.

This guide walks through the whole process — operator registration, the exam, and what changes after you pass.

## Who actually needs A1/A3

You need the A1/A3 certificate if **any** of the following applies:

- Your drone weighs **250 g or more**
- Your drone has a **sensor capable of capturing personal data** (including any camera) — this catches almost every consumer drone, even those under 250 g such as the DJI Mini series
- You fly under the **Open category** at all (it is the default category for hobby and light commercial flight)

If you are flying a sub-250 g toy drone without a camera over your own garden with nobody else around, technically you do not need A1/A3. In practice, the moment you mount a camera or take it to a park, you do.

## Step 1 — Register as a UAS operator

Before you do anything else, register on the Latvian CAA portal at **e.caa.gov.lv**. You are registering yourself as a **UAS operator** — the person legally responsible for the drone — not the drone itself.

What you need:

- A valid Latvian electronic ID (eID, eParaksts mobile, or Smart-ID)
- A valid email address
- Civil liability insurance details (for commercial operations only — see [our insurance article](#) when it's published)

After registration you receive an **operator number** in the form `LVAxxxxxxxxxxxx`. You must mark this number visibly on every drone you fly, and enter it into the drone's Remote ID field if the drone supports one (most C-class drones do).

This step takes about 10 minutes. There is no fee.

## Step 2 — Study the theory

The A1/A3 exam covers **nine topic areas** defined by EASA:

1. Air safety
2. Airspace limitations
3. Aviation regulation
4. Human performance
5. Operational procedures
6. UAS general knowledge
7. Privacy and data protection
8. Insurance
9. Security

You can study these directly from the [EASA Easy Access Rules](https://www.easa.europa.eu/) — but the official document runs to ~430 pages of regulatory text. Most candidates use a structured course instead.

**[dronelingo's free Air Safety topic](/learn/air-safety)** gives you a feel for the depth required. The full curriculum covers all nine topics with practice drills and an unlimited mock exam.

The exam itself is **40 multiple-choice questions, 40 minutes, ≥75 % to pass**. You may retake it as many times as you need; each attempt is free.

## Step 3 — Take the online exam

The exam is hosted on the same **e.caa.gov.lv** portal where you registered.

What to expect:

- Each question has four options. Exactly one is correct.
- A timer counts down from 40 minutes in the upper-right corner.
- You can navigate between questions and change answers until you submit.
- The system marks the exam immediately. You see your score, which topics you got wrong, and whether you passed.

If you fail, the system lets you start a fresh attempt straight away — though most people benefit from a few days of focused practice on their weak topics first. The [topic drills on dronelingo](/exam) let you target specific areas without doing a full mock each time.

## Step 4 — After you pass

Passing the exam earns you the **A1/A3 Theoretical Knowledge Certificate**. It is valid **for five years**, after which you must re-take the exam to renew it. The certificate is digital — it lives in your e.caa.gov.lv profile and can be downloaded as PDF for printing.

You can now legally fly under A1 and A3 rules:

- **A1**: drones up to ~900 g, in built-up areas, over uninvolved people (but never over crowds)
- **A3**: any drone up to 25 kg, in unpopulated areas, ≥150 m from residential / commercial / recreational areas

You **cannot** yet fly A2 (closer than 30 m horizontal to uninvolved people). A2 requires an additional theory exam plus a self-declaration of practical training. We will cover A2 in a separate article.

## Common mistakes that fail the exam

Three patterns trip up most first-time candidates:

1. **Mistaking VLOS for "I can see roughly where it is."** VLOS means continuous, unaided visual contact with the drone itself — not just knowing its position from the controller screen. FPV flight without a dedicated observer is not VLOS.

2. **Confusing operator and pilot registration.** You register as an **operator**. The pilot (the person actually flying) is a separate role; on small operations they are the same person, but on a film shoot they may differ. The exam tests this distinction directly.

3. **Underestimating airspace limitations.** Riga has a complex mix of airport CTRs, restricted military zones, and protected nature areas. The exam expects you to recognise that "120 m AGL maximum" is a baseline, not a guarantee that you can fly anywhere up to that height.

## Practising for the exam

The fastest way to identify your weak topics is a **diagnostic test** — ten questions, one per topic, instant feedback. dronelingo's [free diagnostic at /exam/diagnostic](/exam) gives you a baseline in under five minutes.

After the diagnostic, work the daily warm-up: it surfaces questions you got wrong first, then fresh questions from your weak topics. Most candidates need **three to five days** of 15-minute sessions to reach a consistent ≥80 % on full mock exams — the readiness threshold we recommend before sitting the real exam.

## Cost summary

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Operator registration | Free |
| Theory exam (each attempt) | Free |
| A1/A3 certificate | Free |
| Drone-specific markings (sticker / engraver) | ~€5 one-time |
| **Total to fly legally** | **~€5** |

Latvia is one of the cheapest EASA states for getting started. Most cost lives in the time you spend studying — the exam will quietly cost you a week of evenings if you walk in cold.
