
Latvia is not sourcing German or American hardware for its eastern border drone problem. It is deploying its own — interceptor drones from Latvian manufacturers Origin Robotics and Eraser, mounted on mobile teams in 4x4 vehicles, with up to four soldiers per unit. The target for operational readiness: early June 2026.

That is a specific industrial signal, not just a security measure. It means Latvia has manufacturers capable of fielding operational counter-drone systems. And it means the local drone ecosystem — from hardware development to operator training — is being shaped by a set of pressures that did not exist two years ago.

## The deployment and its context

On May 26, 2026, Maj. Modris Kairišs, head of Latvia's Autonomous Systems Competence Center, briefed reporters in Riga on the plan. Mobile intercept teams equipped with Origin Robotics and Eraser drones will deploy to the border with Russia within days. Kairišs said the number of teams to be deployed is classified, but the goal is to have initial capability in place by the start of next month.

The backdrop is direct. Independent Russian media outlet Novaya Gazeta Europe counted at least 24 drone incidents in the three Baltic countries since early 2025. On May 7, 2026, two drones crashed in Latvian territory — one struck an empty fuel depot. Latvia's government collapsed in the aftermath.

Latvia's eastern border with Russia and Belarus runs nearly 400 kilometers. Kairišs said that reaching Ukrainian-style levels of drone coverage would require "a huge number" of personnel and place serious stress on the armed forces. The initial mobile teams are a first step toward a capability, not the end state.

## What the kill chain concept means here

Kill chain is a way of describing the sequence from threat detection to neutralization: detect, identify, decide, act. In counter-drone operations, the sequence only works if each step happens fast enough to matter.

Detection is the hardest part. Shahed-type drones fly at 50 to 100 meters altitude and are built from cheap composite materials that are largely transparent to radar. Latvia has deployed a network of acoustic sensors for several months, which Kairišs said — when combined with radar and optical sensors — is "quite effective." But tactical radars for detecting low-flying drones are needed every 10 to 20 kilometers, with the infrastructure that implies along a 400-kilometer line.

Command and control presents a separate structural problem. Kairišs described it as a "legacy way of thinking" built into NATO's C2 architecture: sensor data gets fused and classified at higher levels before it can flow to a mobile team at the tactical edge. That classification overhead disrupts the time-sensitive steps of the kill chain. His conclusion is that inter-drone interception needs to work at "a very low classification level" — and that NATO countries developing their own C2 systems will need to make them interoperable.

## What Ukraine transfers to Latvia

Ukraine has been running mobile anti-drone fire teams since 2022. The country has increasingly shifted from small-arms engagement to interceptor drones as the primary defeat method. Latvia is drawing on Ukrainian expertise — not hardware, but tactical knowledge: kill chain procedures, sensor placement logic, and how to make fast decisions in a dynamic field environment.

That is a rare kind of knowledge. Most European countries are learning counter-UAS from simulations, published doctrine, or short range tests. Ukraine is generating it from operational reality — dozens of intercepts per week, in contested conditions. That experience is now being transferred to Latvia through structured cooperation.

Kairišs also described the next step beyond mobile teams: fully automatic interceptor drones pre-positioned in launch canisters along the border, controlled from a command center with no operator needed at each launch point. Latvia is already testing what he called "launch-box technologies." "This is where Western countries need to move," he said, "because our human resource is very expensive."

## What this means for Latvia's drone industry

Origin Robotics and Eraser are not prototype suppliers. Their systems are going into field deployment — which means production capability, field support structures, and iterative development under operational feedback.

Latvia also hosts NATO's innovation range for counter-UAS at the Sēlija training area. Kairišs pointed out that testing C-UAS systems against realistic threat profiles is "almost impossible" in many European countries — bureaucratic constraints, population density, and air traffic density all get in the way. At Sēlija, Latvia can run electronic warfare tests and high-altitude exercises under special government exemptions. That testing environment is a concrete competitive advantage for local developers.

The Autonomous Systems Competence Center holds special government authority to fast-track procurement — compressing a cycle that normally takes years into months. That changes the product development feedback loop for manufacturers like Origin Robotics and Eraser. It also makes Latvia an unusually practical test environment for the broader NATO C-UAS community.

## Which roles are becoming scarce

The counter-drone cluster creates demand for people with specific competencies. Some of those roles are clearly military. Others are not.

**Drone operators.** The fundamental requirement. Interceptor drone operation demands precise low-altitude control, fast sensor interpretation, and decision-making under pressure. The skill floor is higher than recreational piloting.

**Systems integrators.** Sensor networks, command interfaces, drone avionics — these don't work in isolation. Integration is one of the highest-value roles in any C-UAS architecture, and it requires people who can work across hardware, software, and protocol layers.

**Maintenance and field technicians.** Autonomous systems operated in field conditions fail. Someone has to repair them, reconfigure them, push software updates. This work happens on the range today, and along border support points tomorrow.

**RF and electronic warfare specialists.** Drone detection and suppression is a competition in the electromagnetic spectrum. Interception often includes an EW component — jamming, navigation disruption, or frequency scanning. Demand for this competency is not going away.

**Procurement and project managers.** A fast-tracked acquisition cycle means more frequent tender processes, more technical specifications to write, more test protocols to manage. Someone runs that work.

Some of these roles will emerge at manufacturers, at the range, and at systems integration companies — not only inside the military.

## What it means for civilian drone training

Dronelingo is a platform for EASA A1/A3 certification preparation. The audience is civilian operators — recreational, commercial, or anyone working toward the formal qualification that Latvia requires under European drone regulation. That scope does not change.

What changes is the market context around it.

An operator who understands drone aerodynamics, can read sensor data, and can reason through flight parameters under difficult conditions is worth more than one who passed a test and stopped there. More employers are noticing that distinction — in infrastructure inspection, monitoring, and increasingly in roles adjacent to the C-UAS sector where formal certification and regulatory knowledge are prerequisites, not nice-to-haves.

Latvia as a NATO C-UAS innovation hub also draws more researchers, partner organizations, and international programs. That increases the number of professional contexts where holding a recognized civilian qualification matters. The [certification guide for Latvia](/guide) and the [practice section](/practice) are designed for exactly this — building the kind of grounded competency that holds up in real conditions, not just test conditions.

## The near-term picture

Within a few weeks Latvia went from border drone incidents to fielded intercept teams using domestically-manufactured systems, a NATO testing range, an accelerated procurement authority, and structured access to Ukrainian battlefield expertise. The counter-drone cluster is not a roadmap. It is already operational.

Demand for people who understand drones is rising in a market that was already growing before the security dimension arrived. Not every role will require a military contract. But every serious role in this space — civilian or otherwise — will require the kind of technical fluency that formal certification starts to build.
