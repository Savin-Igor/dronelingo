The operating picture for civilian drone pilots in Latgale shifted materially in May 2026. Two things drove that shift: a Ukrainian drone, diverted by Russian electronic warfare, struck a fuel depot in Rezekne on 7 May — and Latvia's armed forces responded by deploying mobile intercept teams to the eastern border. Both have practical consequences for anyone planning a flight in the region.

Political context is kept brief here. The questions that matter for pilots are operational.

## What happened on 7 May

In the early hours of 7 May 2026, three drones crossed into Latvian airspace from Russia. Two came down on Latvian territory; a third later exited. One struck the fuel depot in Rezekne, roughly 40 km inside the border. It damaged four oil storage tanks. The tanks were empty. No casualties were reported.

French military aircraft operating under NATO's Baltic Air Policing mission were scrambled during the alert. Flight restrictions reaching up to approximately 6 km altitude were imposed across Latvia's eastern border region. They were lifted later that same day.

On 10 May, Latvia confirmed the drones were Ukrainian and had been diverted from their intended targets by Russian electronic warfare. Ukraine's foreign minister publicly linked the incident to that cause.

### The political beat — briefly

Latvia's Defence Minister Andris Sprūds announced his resignation on 10 May. Prime Minister Evika Siliņa's government collapsed on 14 May. These are on record here for completeness; they do not change the operating environment directly.

## What is now on the ground

On 27 May 2026, Latvia's armed forces announced mobile intercept teams for the eastern border: four soldiers in 4x4 vehicles, carrying interceptor drones from Latvian manufacturers Origin Robotics and Eraser. Target operational date: early next month. Named source: Major Modris Kairišs, head of the Autonomous Systems Competence Center.

The eastern border with Russia and Belarus runs approximately 400 km. These teams are mobile — their position changes. You cannot predict from a map where they will be on a given day.

One additional number: Novaya Gazeta Europe, an independent Russian outlet, has counted at least 24 drone incidents across the three Baltic states since early 2025. Rezekne is not an isolated event.

## Our reading for civilian pilots

The following is our operational interpretation, not official guidance from CAA Latvia or the armed forces.

### The NOTAM picture has changed

The 7 May restrictions — up to 6 km altitude, imposed and lifted the same day — are a precedent. Airspace can close in the eastern region with very little lead time. Your sub-120 m EASA Open Category flight sits well below any altitude cap of that scale, but the airspace can close entirely, not just above a threshold.

This means a NOTAM check from the day before is no longer sufficient when operating in Latgale. Check NOTAMs on the morning of the flight, as close to takeoff as practical. Events that affect your area can land overnight.

### Intercept teams operate their own drones

Mobile intercept teams carry interceptor drones — active assets in the same airspace you fly in. The teams operate in the border zone, but that zone is not precisely delineated in the public announcement. Treat Latgale as a more contested environment than it was three months ago. That is not a reason to avoid the region; it is a reason to build a tighter routine.

### Recommended additions to your pre-flight checklist

- **Re-check NOTAMs same morning.** Restrictions can appear overnight or within hours of a developing situation.
- **Verify your alert-broadcast capability.** If you fly with a phone on your person, confirm that emergency cell-broadcast alerts are enabled. Latvia's NBS now uses a two-tier Yellow/Orange system for airspace threats. If your device cannot receive it, you are flying without a warning channel.
- **Have an abort SOP ready before takeoff.** If civil ATC declares a restriction in your sector while you are airborne, you need a pre-planned response, not an improvised one. Know your nearest safe landing zone before you launch.

### If you encounter unfamiliar wreckage while flying

Land, log, and call 112. Do not approach any debris on the ground or any unfamiliar low-flying object from the air. This is standard civilian guidance in Latvia and applies whether you are on foot or operating a drone.

## What this does not change

The legal framework for EASA Open Category operations in Latvia has not been amended. CAA Latvia authorizations, subcategory rules, and standard pre-flight requirements remain unchanged. What has shifted is the risk profile of the operating environment — not the regulatory text.

## The new baseline for Latgale flights

Operating in Latgale is more demanding than it was before May 2026. The additions to your routine are modest and do not require new equipment. They require more attention to the pre-flight window and a practiced abort SOP.

- Check NOTAMs same morning.
- Confirm alert reception on your phone before takeoff.
- Know your abort procedure and your nearest landing zone before you are airborne.
- Log any alert or restriction you encounter, with time and coordinates.
