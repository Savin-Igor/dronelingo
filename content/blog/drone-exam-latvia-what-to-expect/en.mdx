The public facts about the Latvian A1/A3 exam are straightforward: it is online, free, timed, and based on the nine Open-category knowledge areas published by the CAA.

## Official format

The CAA A1/A3 course page states:

- **40 questions**
- **40 minutes**
- at least **75% correct** to pass

The course itself is **free**, and the certificate issued after a successful pass is valid for **5 years** across the **European Union**.

## What the exam is built around

The published syllabus covers nine topics:

1. air safety
2. airspace limitations
3. aviation regulation
4. human performance limitations
5. operational procedures
6. general UAS knowledge
7. privacy and data protection
8. insurance
9. security

That matters because the safest way to prepare is to study by topic, not to chase rumours about “the real questions”.

## What you need before you start

To enter the Latvian A1/A3 course and exam, the public CAA page lists one practical prerequisite:

- a Latvian **remote-pilot number**

In practice, that means you need portal access at `e.caa.gov.lv` before you can sit the exam.

## What to expect on the day

The official page does not describe a complicated process. The basic sequence is simple:

1. log into the CAA portal
2. open the A1/A3 course and exam section
3. complete the exam within the published time limit
4. after a successful pass, access the certificate in the CAA UAS information system

The important part is not the interface. The important part is whether you can apply the Open-category rules cleanly under time pressure.

## What is worth revising carefully

The topics that usually deserve a second reading are the ones people think they already understand:

- the difference between **operator** and **remote pilot**
- when **registration** is required
- **VLOS** versus FPV
- the **120 m** height limit
- the difference between **A1, A2 and A3**
- Latvian **geographical zones**
- basic privacy, insurance, and security obligations

If your understanding is only verbal — “I roughly know how it works” — that usually shows up in the exam.

## Better preparation strategy

A practical approach is:

1. complete the official CAA course once without rushing
2. review the rules for **Open category** and **registration**
3. revisit the numeric thresholds and distance rules
4. practise scenario questions only after the rules themselves are clear

That sequence is slower at the start, but it is usually faster than trying to memorise answer patterns.

## What not to rely on

Avoid three weak strategies:

- treating blog summaries as if they were the regulation
- learning isolated answers without understanding the rule behind them
- assuming that a sub-250 g camera drone and an A1/A3 requirement are always the same thing

The official CAA pages are more reliable than recycled “exam tips”.
