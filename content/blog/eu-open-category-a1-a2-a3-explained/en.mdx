Most drone pilots in Latvia fly in the **Open category**. Inside that category, the rules are split into **A1, A2, and A3**. The differences matter because they decide three things at once:

- which aircraft you can use
- how close you may fly to uninvolved people
- which qualification you need

## The short comparison

| Subcategory | Main idea | Aircraft most commonly used | Pilot qualification |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A1** | lighter aircraft, closest to people | `<250 g`, `C0`, `C1` | A1/A3 where required |
| **A2** | closer operations with a `C2` aircraft | `C2` | A1/A3 + A2 |
| **A3** | flights far from people and built-up areas | `C0` to `C4`, depending on the case | A1/A3 |

## A1

A1 is the least restrictive subcategory in relation to uninvolved people, but it does **not** allow flight over crowds.

In practice:

- aircraft below `250 g` and `C0` aircraft may be used in A1
- `C1` aircraft may also be used in A1
- with `C1`, the remote pilot must **not intentionally overfly** uninvolved people

So A1 is not “fly anywhere”. It is simply the Open-category subcategory with the lightest aircraft and the smallest separation from people.

## A2

A2 is the middle ground. It exists for operations with a **C2** aircraft closer to uninvolved people than A3 would allow.

The core distance rule is:

- at least `30 m` horizontally from uninvolved people
- this may be reduced when the low-speed mode conditions are met, but the distance logic still remains restrictive

For Latvia, the public CAA materials describe A2 as a separate path:

1. valid **A1/A3** proof first
2. practical self-training
3. separate **A2** exam

## A3

A3 is the subcategory for operations **far from uninvolved people** and away from residential, commercial, industrial, and recreational areas.

The CAA Open-category summary highlights two points:

- the flight must stay at least `150 m` horizontally from those areas
- the operation must also respect the distance logic from uninvolved people inside the operating area

A3 often becomes the fallback for heavier or older aircraft when A1 or A2 does not fit.

## Which certificate goes with which subcategory

The qualification structure is simpler than many summaries make it sound:

- one **A1/A3** certificate covers both **A1** and **A3**
- **A2** requires the additional A2 step

There is no separate “A1-only” or “A3-only” certificate.

## What about drones under 250 g?

This is the point most often oversimplified online.

For aircraft under `250 g`:

- operator registration may still be required, especially when the aircraft carries a camera and is not a toy
- but operator registration and A1/A3 qualification are **not the same requirement**

So the right question is not “Does it have a camera?” on its own. The right question is:

- what is the aircraft class or mass?
- which subcategory applies?
- does the CAA require pilot qualification for that case?

## Legacy aircraft

The transition period has ended. The Latvian CAA summary explains that older drones without a C-class label may still be used, but their operational options are narrower than for class-marked aircraft.

For legacy aircraft, it is worth checking:

- the aircraft mass
- whether the operation fits A1 or only A3
- whether a newer class-marked aircraft would make the intended operation easier

## A good way to read Open

If you want to stay out of trouble, do not start with product names or marketing labels. Start with:

1. **subcategory**
2. **aircraft class or mass**
3. **distance to uninvolved people**
4. **geographical-zone restrictions**

Once those four are clear, most Open-category decisions become much easier.
