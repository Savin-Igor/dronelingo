In Latvia, “Can I fly here?” is not a question you answer from memory. The official answer is map-based and can change during the day. That is why the first rule is simple: check the official source before every flight.

## The official source

The Latvian CAA page on UAS geographical zones states that the **only official and legally binding source** for airspace restrictions in Latvia is:

- `https://www.airspace.lv/drones/`

The same CAA page also warns that manufacturer geo-awareness functions may be incomplete or outdated. So an app on the controller is a convenience layer, not the legal answer.

## What kinds of zones exist

The CAA explains several UAS geographical-zone types:

| Zone type | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Informational** | additional awareness, but no extra restriction by itself |
| **Restrictive — authorisation** | the flight must be coordinated or authorised before take-off |
| **Restrictive — notification** | the flight must be notified |
| **Restrictive — conditions** | extra technical, operational, or pilot conditions apply |
| **Prohibited** | flights are not allowed unless a specific exception exists |

If several zones overlap, the conditions stack. In practice, that means the safest reading is the strictest one that applies at your location.

## Approval does not cancel the basic rules

The CAA also states something many pilots misunderstand: approval inside a geographical zone does **not** cancel the basic Open-category rules.

For example, zone approval does not automatically let you ignore:

- the **120 m** height limit
- the **VLOS** requirement
- the other limits that still apply to your category and aircraft

Zone approval solves the zone-specific restriction. It does not erase the rest of the rule set.

## BGKIS requirement from 2025

The published CAA guidance says that from `2025-01-01`, flights in UAS geographical zones must be registered in **BGKIS** before the flight or flight series, where that requirement applies.

That means the practical workflow is not just “look at the map and go”. In many places, the map check and the formal flight submission are part of the same legal routine.

## Protected areas and local rules

Airspace is not the only layer. In protected nature areas, extra environmental rules may apply alongside the aviation rules. Before filming in national parks, reserves, wetlands, or other protected zones, check whether the site manager or nature-protection authority requires an additional permit or imposes local restrictions.

The safe approach is simple: if the place is environmentally sensitive, do not assume that an aviation-only check is enough.

## A better way to check before take-off

Before every flight:

1. open `airspace.lv/drones`
2. check what zone type applies at the exact location
3. confirm whether BGKIS notification or authorisation is required
4. then confirm that the flight still fits your Open-category limits

The CAA specifically reminds pilots that airspace information can change **several times a day**. Yesterday’s answer is not today’s answer.

## What not to do

Avoid three habits:

- relying only on the drone manufacturer’s map
- assuming that a zone approval lets you ignore height or VLOS rules
- treating protected nature areas as a pure aviation question

That is how legal flights turn into preventable violations.
