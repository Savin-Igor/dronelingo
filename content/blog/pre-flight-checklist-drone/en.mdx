A pre-flight checklist matters for one reason: it forces you to check the things that are easy to skip when you are in a hurry.

This version stays deliberately simple. It focuses on the checks that remain relevant across ordinary Open-category flights in Latvia.

## 1. Weather

Confirm that conditions are suitable for the aircraft and for **VLOS**:

- wind within the aircraft’s practical limit
- no precipitation that would make the flight unsafe
- visibility clear enough to keep the drone in unaided sight

## 2. Airspace and geographical zones

Check the official Latvian source:

- `airspace.lv/drones`

Then confirm whether the location is:

- open without extra restrictions
- subject to notification
- subject to authorisation
- prohibited

If the zone requires action through BGKIS or another approval step, that comes before take-off.

## 3. Category fit

Before flying, confirm that the planned operation still fits the intended Open-category case:

- correct subcategory
- correct aircraft class or mass
- correct distance logic from uninvolved people

That quick mental check prevents many avoidable mistakes.

## 4. Take-off and landing area

Check that the launch point and recovery point are both suitable:

- stable surface
- enough space
- no obvious conflict with uninvolved people
- a realistic option to stop the flight early if conditions change

## 5. Aircraft condition

Inspect the aircraft itself:

- airframe condition
- propellers
- battery seating
- payload attachment, if any

If anything looks loose, damaged, swollen, or improvised, the safe decision is not to launch.

## 6. Controller, link, and positioning

Confirm that the basic control chain is healthy:

- controller battery
- aircraft battery
- expected signal environment
- positioning status where relevant to the operation

This is not about chasing a perfect screen. It is about confirming that the aircraft is ready for the type of flight you are about to conduct.

## 7. Operator number and identification

If the aircraft and operation require it, check that:

- the aircraft is marked with the operator number
- the required identification settings are correctly entered

That is easiest to verify before the motors start, not after.

## 8. Documents and qualification

Where relevant, confirm that the required paperwork is in order:

- operator registration
- pilot qualification
- insurance documentation, if the operation requires it

This is especially important if you are moving between different aircraft or different types of operation.

## 9. Local environment

Look up from the screen and assess the real scene:

- people entering the area
- animals
- vehicles
- obstacles
- overhead wires, masts, cranes, or trees

The map is not the whole environment.

## 10. Emergency logic

Before take-off, decide what you will do if:

- the signal becomes unstable
- an uninvolved person enters the area
- another aircraft appears
- the flight no longer matches the original plan

If the abort plan is vague, the pre-flight check is not finished.

## 11. Mission discipline

Keep the intended flight simple:

- what is the purpose?
- what is the route?
- what is the stop condition?

Short, clear flights are easier to fly safely than improvised ones.

## 12. Final no-go decision

Right before launch, ask one last question:

- if something changes in the first minute, am I already too committed?

If the honest answer is yes, the setup is probably too tight.
