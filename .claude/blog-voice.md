# Blog Voice — dronelingo tech features

## Purpose

This voice spec applies to long-form blog posts about drones, UAV systems, counter-UAS, inspection, and modern autonomous aviation technologies.

## Core voice

- **Editorial, not promotional.**
- **Technically literate, but readable.**
- **Direct and contemporary.**
- **Sceptical of hype.**

The writing should read closer to a strong analyst piece in `Defense News`, `DroneXL`, `The War Zone`, or a well-edited industry brief than to vendor marketing or a generic SEO article.

## What the blog sounds like

- Opens with the question the article answers, not with background filler.
- Explains systems through **trade-offs, constraints, economics, and operational fit**.
- Uses short and medium-length sentences most of the time, then occasionally a longer sentence to connect ideas.
- Prefers **plain technical wording** over inflated jargon.
- Uses lists and comparison tables when they reduce ambiguity.
- Names technologies, payloads, programs, and manufacturers precisely.
- Treats “future” claims carefully: distinguish between **fielded**, **testing**, **pilot deployment**, and **announced**.

## Perspective

- Default perspective: **third-person editorial**.
- Use `you` only when helping the reader make a decision, compare options, or avoid a common mistake.
- Avoid `we` unless used in a short CTA or navigation line.

## Tone rules

- Confident, but never absolute without evidence.
- Analytical, not breathless.
- Concrete, not abstract.
- Calm around military topics: no dramatization, no triumphalism, no slogan-like phrasing.
- Avoid moralizing and avoid turning operational examples into entertainment.

## Structure rules

- First paragraph must state the problem, shift, or market question.
- H2s should be declarative and useful in isolation.
- H3s should break down comparisons, architectures, workflows, or adoption barriers.
- End with one short takeaway section that tells the reader what matters now.
- FAQ answers must be compact and factual.

## Language rules

- Do not explain basic drone terminology like a school textbook.
- Do not define common acronyms the audience already knows unless the meaning is operationally important in context.
- Avoid SEO sludge like:
  - “In today’s fast-paced world”
  - “game-changer” unless quoted or specifically justified
  - “revolutionary” unless tied to a concrete capability shift
  - “cutting-edge” as filler
- Avoid vendor-speak like:
  - “seamless”
  - “best-in-class” unless benchmarked
  - “unlocks new possibilities”

## Evidence rules

- No fabricated numbers, contracts, deliveries, or combat claims.
- If a capability is **announced** rather than operational, say so.
- Prefer naming the program, aircraft, payload, or software layer rather than making vague claims.
- Economic claims must be framed through observable drivers: procurement cost, attrition tolerance, labor reduction, inspection interval, downtime, fuel savings, or detection performance.

## SEO rules

- Primary keyword appears naturally in:
  - SEO title
  - H1
  - first 100 words
  - at least one H2
- Use related terms and synonyms naturally; do not stuff exact match phrases.
- Internal links should be descriptive and useful, not appended mechanically.

## Locale rule

- English is the master technical draft.
- Latvian and Russian versions must read as **native editorial pieces**, not as literal translations.
- Preserve meaning, examples, structure, and factual claims across locales, but allow sentence rhythm and idiom to change naturally.
